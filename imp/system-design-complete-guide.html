Module 01

Core Components of System Design

Before designing anything, you need to know the "Lego blocks" that every large system is built from. Almost every app you use — Instagram, Uber, Netflix — is just these same blocks arranged differently. Let's meet each block.

Client Applications (Web, Mobile, Desktop)

Simple Definition: The client is the part of the system the user actually touches — the website in your browser, the app on your phone, or a desktop program. It collects what you type or tap, shows results, and talks to the backend over the internet. A web client runs in a browser, a mobile client is an installed app (Android/iOS), and a desktop client is software like Slack for Mac. The client's main job is presentation and capturing user actions, not heavy thinking.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA TV remote is the "client." It doesn't play the show — it just sends your button presses to the TV (the backend) which does the real work.

Real-World Tech Example: Instagram has three clients (web at instagram.com, iOS app, Android app) all talking to the same backend.

Why It Matters: The client shapes the user's first impression and decides how much work happens on the device vs the server. A well-designed client reduces unnecessary network calls, works on slow connections, and keeps sensitive logic on the server. Designers must support many client types from one backend.

⚠️ Interview TipNever trust the client — always re-validate data on the server, because anyone can fake requests coming from a "client."

Backend Servers

Simple Definition: A backend server is a computer (usually in a data center) that receives requests from clients, runs the "business logic," talks to databases, and sends back answers. Business logic is the set of rules of your app — e.g., "a user can't withdraw more money than they have." Servers don't have a screen for users; they just process and respond. You usually run many identical servers so the load can be spread out.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA restaurant kitchen: you (client) place an order, the kitchen (server) does the cooking using ingredients (database), and sends the dish back.

Why It Matters: Servers are where most logic and cost live. Keeping them stateless (not storing user-specific memory) lets you add or remove servers freely, which is the foundation of scaling.

Databases

Simple Definition: A database is organized, permanent storage for your data — users, posts, orders, payments. It lets you save (write), look up (read), update, and delete information reliably, even after a server restarts. Databases also enforce rules and keep data consistent. They come in two big families: SQL (tables) and NoSQL (flexible formats), covered in Modules 9 and 10.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA giant, perfectly organized filing cabinet where every document has a label and you can instantly find any file.

Real-World Tech Example: WhatsApp stores chats and contacts; Amazon stores products and orders in databases.

Why It Matters: The database is often the hardest part to scale because data must stay correct. Most big system-design problems are really "how do we store and read this data fast and safely?"

Load Balancers

Simple Definition: A load balancer sits in front of many servers and spreads incoming requests evenly among them. If one server is busy or dead, it sends traffic to the healthy ones. This prevents any single server from being overwhelmed and keeps the app online if a server crashes. (Full detail in Module 13.)

💡 Real-Life ExampleA supermarket receptionist directing customers to whichever checkout counter is free, so no single line gets too long.

Why It Matters: Without it, you can't use multiple servers effectively, and one failure takes down the whole app. It's the entry point that makes horizontal scaling possible.

Message Queues

Simple Definition: A message queue is a buffer that holds tasks so they can be processed later, in the background, instead of making the user wait. The server drops a "to-do" message into the queue and replies instantly; separate worker programs pick up the messages and do the slow work. This is called asynchronous processing. (Full detail in Module 17.)

💡 Real-Life ExampleA restaurant order-ticket rail: waiters clip orders up, and the kitchen works through them at its own pace without the waiter standing and waiting.

Real-World Tech Example: When you upload a video to YouTube, a queue holds the "convert this video" task while you keep browsing.

Why It Matters: Queues absorb traffic spikes, decouple components, and let slow jobs happen without freezing the user experience.

Caching Layers

Simple Definition: A cache is super-fast temporary storage that keeps copies of frequently used data so you don't have to fetch it from the slow database every time. It usually lives in memory (RAM), which is far faster than disk. Caches make apps feel instant and reduce database load. (Full detail in Module 12.)

💡 Real-Life ExampleKeeping your most-used books on your desk instead of walking to the library every single time you need them.

Real-World Tech Example: Twitter caches popular tweets and timelines in Redis so they load in milliseconds.

Why It Matters: Caching is the cheapest, fastest way to boost performance. But stale data (old copies) is a real risk, so designers must plan how to refresh it.

CDN (Content Delivery Network)

Simple Definition: A CDN is a network of servers spread across the world that store copies of static files (images, videos, CSS) close to users. When you load a page, files come from a nearby CDN server instead of the faraway origin, so they arrive faster. (Full detail in Module 6.)

💡 Real-Life ExampleInstead of one central pizza kitchen serving the whole country, you open local branches in every city so delivery is quick.

Real-World Tech Example: Netflix uses a huge CDN so your movie streams from a server in your own city.

Why It Matters: It cuts latency, saves bandwidth on your main servers, and handles huge traffic for media-heavy apps.

API Gateway

Simple Definition: An API gateway is a single front door for all client requests. It routes each request to the right backend service, and handles shared concerns like authentication (who are you?), rate limiting (don't spam us), and logging — all in one place. (Full detail in Module 6.)

💡 Real-Life ExampleA building's security reception: every visitor enters through one desk that checks IDs and directs them to the correct office.

Why It Matters: It keeps individual services simple by centralizing cross-cutting tasks, and gives clients one clean address to call.

Monitoring and Logs

Simple Definition: Monitoring collects numbers about your system's health (CPU, errors, response time), and logs are timestamped records of what happened. Together they let you see problems, get alerts, and investigate why something broke. Without them, you're flying blind. (Full detail in Module 23.)

💡 Real-Life ExampleA car dashboard (speed, fuel, engine light) plus the black-box recorder that explains what went wrong after a problem.

Real-World Tech Example: Companies use Prometheus + Grafana for metrics and the ELK stack or Datadog for logs.

📌 Key TakeawayEvery big system = Clients → (CDN / Gateway / Load Balancer) → Servers → (Cache + Database + Queues), all watched by Monitoring. Master these 9 blocks and every architecture becomes readable.

Module 02

Data-Intensive vs Compute-Intensive Applications

Every system has a "bottleneck" — the thing that runs out first. For some apps it's moving and storing lots of data; for others it's heavy number-crunching. Knowing which kind you're building tells you where to spend money and effort.

What makes an application data-intensive

Simple Definition: A data-intensive app's main challenge is the amount, speed, and complexity of data — not the calculations. The hard parts are storing huge volumes, reading/writing fast, keeping data consistent, and moving it around. The CPU is rarely the limit; databases, networks, and disks are. Most web apps you use daily are data-intensive.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA massive library: the challenge isn't "thinking hard," it's storing millions of books and finding the right one instantly.

Real-World Tech Example: Instagram — billions of photos to store, serve, and search; very little heavy math.

What makes an application compute-intensive

Simple Definition: A compute-intensive app's main challenge is heavy calculation — the CPU or GPU is the bottleneck. Data may be small, but processing it takes enormous computing power. Think simulations, video rendering, AI training, and scientific computing. Here you optimize processors, not databases.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA chess grandmaster: little "data," but enormous thinking per move.

Real-World Tech Example: Training ChatGPT, Pixar rendering a movie frame, or weather-forecast simulations.

Instagram Architecture Discussion

Instagram is a classic data-intensive system. Its hard problems are: storing billions of photos (object storage + CDN), generating each user's feed quickly (caching + fan-out), and handling read-heavy traffic (far more people view photos than post them). Very little of Instagram is heavy computation — it's about moving data fast and reliably to millions of people. This is why they lean on caching, read replicas, and CDNs rather than supercomputers.

Machine Learning and Rendering Workloads

Simple Definition: ML training and 3D/video rendering are compute-intensive. Training a model means doing trillions of math operations over data; rendering means computing the color of every pixel in every frame. Both crave raw processing power and are usually run as long batch jobs on specialized hardware.

Real-World Tech Example: Netflix renders/encodes each title into many formats; Tesla trains self-driving models on GPU clusters.

CPU-bound and GPU-bound Systems

Simple Definition: A system is CPU-bound when the processor is the limit (e.g., complex logic, encryption), and GPU-bound when graphics/AI math dominates. GPUs do thousands of simple operations in parallel, perfect for ML and graphics. Knowing which you are tells you what hardware to buy.

⚠️ Interview TipIf adding more RAM/disk/DB power doesn't help but the CPU stays at 100%, you're CPU-bound — scale compute, not storage.

Real-World Scalability Concerns

Data-intensive systems scale by adding storage, caches, replicas, and shards. Compute-intensive systems scale by adding more/faster CPUs and GPUs, and by parallelizing work. The mistake is treating every problem the same way — throwing database power at a CPU problem (or vice versa) wastes money and doesn't fix the bottleneck.

OLTP vs OLAP Workloads

Simple Definition: OLTP (Online Transaction Processing) handles many small, fast operations — placing an order, liking a post. OLAP (Online Analytical Processing) handles few but huge analytical queries over lots of data — "total sales per region last year." OLTP is the live app; OLAP is the reporting/analytics brain.

💡 Real-Life ExampleOLTP = a cashier ringing up individual purchases. OLAP = the manager studying a year of sales to find trends.

Batch vs Real-Time Processing Workloads

Simple Definition: Batch processing collects data and processes it in big chunks on a schedule (e.g., nightly). Real-time (stream) processing handles each event the moment it arrives, within milliseconds to seconds. Batch is cheaper and simpler; real-time is needed when freshness matters (fraud alerts, live dashboards).

💡 Real-Life ExampleBatch = doing all your laundry once a week. Real-time = washing each shirt the instant it gets dirty.
📌 Key TakeawayFirst ask: "Is my bottleneck data or compute?" and "Do I need answers now (real-time/OLTP) or later in bulk (batch/OLAP)?" These two questions steer your entire design.

Module 03

Functional vs Non-Functional Requirements

When designing a system, requirements come in two flavors: what it does (functional) and how well it does it (non-functional). Beginners obsess over features; great designers obsess over the "how well." Let's cover both.

Functional Requirements

Simple Definition: Functional requirements describe the actual features and behaviors — what the system must do. "Users can sign up," "users can post a photo," "users can search." They answer the question, "What can a user accomplish here?" They're usually easy to list and directly visible to users.

💡 Real-Life ExampleFor a car: "it must drive, brake, and turn." Those are functional requirements.
flowchart TD R["📋 Requirements"] --> F["✅ Functional
(What it does)"] R --> N["⚙️ Non-Functional
(How well it does it)"] F --> F1["Sign up · Post · Search · Pay"] N --> N1["Scalability · Availability · Reliability"] N --> N2["Performance · Security · Maintainability"] N --> N3["Observability · Cost · Extensibility"]

The following are non-functional requirements (NFRs) — the qualities that make a system production-ready. Often abbreviated as the "-ilities."

Scalability

Simple Definition: The ability to handle growth — more users, more data, more traffic — by adding resources, without redesigning everything. A scalable system serves 100 users and 100 million users using the same basic design, just bigger.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA highway that can add more lanes as traffic grows, instead of being rebuilt from scratch.

Availability

Simple Definition: The percentage of time the system is up and usable. Measured in "nines" — 99.9% ("three nines") means about 8.7 hours of downtime per year; 99.99% means under an hour. High availability comes from redundancy and removing single points of failure.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA 24/7 hospital that always has staff on duty — never closed, no matter what.

Reliability

Simple Definition: The system does the correct thing consistently and doesn't lose or corrupt data, even when parts fail. Availability means "it's up"; reliability means "and it works correctly." A reliable bank never loses your deposit.

⚠️ Interview TipAvailability ≠ Reliability. A site can be "up" (available) but returning wrong data (unreliable). Mention both.

Performance

Simple Definition: How fast the system responds (latency) and how much it can handle per second (throughput). Users notice delays above ~100ms. Good performance means low latency under realistic load, not just when the system is empty.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA fast-food drive-through measured by how quickly you get your food (latency) and how many cars it serves per hour (throughput).

Security

Simple Definition: Protecting data and access — making sure only the right people can do the right things, and data is safe from theft or tampering. Covers authentication, authorization, encryption, and defense against attacks. (Module 22 goes deep.)

Maintainability

Simple Definition: How easily engineers can understand, fix, and change the system over time. Clean code, modular design, and good docs make a system maintainable. Poor maintainability means every change is risky and slow.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA car designed so a mechanic can reach every part easily, vs one where changing a bulb means removing the engine.

Observability

Simple Definition: How well you can understand what's happening inside the system from the outside — through logs, metrics, and traces. An observable system tells you why it's slow or broken, not just that it is. (Module 23.)

Cost Efficiency

Simple Definition: Getting the needed performance and reliability without overspending. Every replica, cache, and region costs money. Good design balances quality against budget instead of buying maximum everything. (Module 25.)

Extensibility

Simple Definition: How easily you can add new features without breaking existing ones. An extensible system has clean interfaces so new modules plug in cleanly. It's about future growth in capability, not just traffic.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA phone with expandable storage and an app store — you add new abilities without replacing the phone.
📌 Key TakeawayIn interviews, always nail functional requirements first, then explicitly discuss the key NFRs (usually scalability, availability, latency, consistency). The NFRs drive every architectural choice you'll make.

Module 04

Scalability Patterns

Scalability is the art of handling more load gracefully. Here are the core patterns and tools you'll reach for again and again.

Vertical Scaling (Scale Up)

Simple Definition: Make one machine more powerful — add more CPU, RAM, or disk. It's simple (no code changes) and great for getting started. But there's a ceiling: a single machine can only get so big, and it's a single point of failure.

💡 Real-Life ExampleUpgrading from a bicycle to a motorbike to a truck — one vehicle, made stronger.

Horizontal Scaling (Scale Out)

Simple Definition: Add more machines and split the work among them. There's no real ceiling — you can keep adding servers — and it removes the single point of failure. The catch: your app must be designed to run on many machines, which needs load balancers and stateless services.

💡 Real-Life ExampleInstead of one giant truck, use a fleet of many delivery vans working together.
flowchart LR subgraph V["⬆️ Vertical (Scale Up)"] v1["Small Server"] --> v2["Bigger Server"] --> v3["Huge Server"] end subgraph H["➡️ Horizontal (Scale Out)"] LB["⚖️ Load Balancer"] --> h1["Server"] LB --> h2["Server"] LB --> h3["Server"] end
Vertical = one bigger box. Horizontal = many boxes.

Real-World Tech Example: Google and Netflix scale horizontally across tens of thousands of commodity servers.

Stateless vs Stateful Services

Simple Definition: A stateless service keeps no memory of past requests on the server — every request carries all it needs, so any server can handle it. A stateful service remembers things (like a session) on a specific server, so requests must return to that same server. Stateless is the key to easy horizontal scaling.

💡 Real-Life ExampleStateless = any cashier can serve you because you carry your shopping list. Stateful = only the one cashier who remembers your order can help.
⚠️ Interview TipPush state out of servers into a shared store (Redis/DB). Then servers stay stateless and you can add/remove them freely.

Auto-scaling

Simple Definition: Automatically adding servers when traffic rises and removing them when it falls, based on metrics like CPU. You save money during quiet times and survive spikes without manual work. Cloud providers (AWS, GCP) offer this out of the box.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA store opening extra checkout counters at rush hour and closing them at night.

Consistent Hashing

Simple Definition: A clever technique to decide which server (or cache node) stores which piece of data, so that when you add or remove a server, only a small fraction of data moves — not everything. Plain hashing (key % N) reshuffles almost all data when N changes; consistent hashing avoids that by placing servers and keys on a virtual ring.

💡 Real-Life ExampleSeating guests around a round table: adding one new chair only shifts the few guests nearest to it, not the entire table.

keys between C and D"] -.-> Ring
Keys map to the next node clockwise on the ring; adding a node moves minimal data.

Real-World Tech Example: Used by DynamoDB, Cassandra, and Redis Cluster to distribute data across nodes.

Hot vs Cold Data Separation

Simple Definition: Hot data is accessed often and recently (today's posts); cold data is rarely touched (5-year-old logs). Keep hot data on fast, expensive storage (RAM/SSD) and move cold data to cheap, slow storage. This saves money while keeping common operations fast.

💡 Real-Life ExampleClothes you wear weekly stay in your closet (hot); winter coats go to the attic in summer (cold).

Read-heavy vs Write-heavy Optimization

Simple Definition: Some systems are read-heavy (many more reads than writes, like a news site) and some are write-heavy (like a logging or IoT system). Read-heavy systems benefit from caching and read replicas; write-heavy systems benefit from sharding, batching writes, and write-optimized databases.

Back-of-Envelope Capacity Estimation

Simple Definition: Quick, rough math to size a system before building — estimating requests per second, storage, and bandwidth using simple assumptions. You're not aiming for precision, just the right order of magnitude (thousands? millions?). It tells you whether one server or a thousand is needed.

💡 Real-Life ExamplePlanning a party: "50 guests × 2 slices each = 100 slices = ~13 pizzas." Rough but good enough to shop.

A worked example (Twitter-like):

Users: 300 million total, 50% daily active = 150M DAU
Tweets: each user posts 2/day → 300M tweets/day
Writes/sec = 300,000,000 / 86,400 sec ≈ 3,500 writes/sec
Reads are ~100× writes → ~350,000 reads/sec  (read-heavy!)
Storage: 300M tweets × 300 bytes ≈ 90 GB/day ≈ 33 TB/year (text only)
⚠️ Interview TipMemorize: 1 day ≈ 86,400 sec (~10⁵). "Per day ÷ 100,000 ≈ per second." This single trick speeds up every estimation.
📌 Key TakeawayPrefer horizontal scaling + stateless services + caching for read-heavy apps. Use consistent hashing to distribute data, separate hot/cold data to control cost, and always do quick capacity math first.

Module 05

DNS (Domain Name System)

DNS is the internet's phonebook. It turns a human-friendly name like google.com into the numeric IP address computers actually use to find each other. Let's trace exactly how it works.

💡 Real-Life ExampleYour phone's contacts app: you tap "Mom," and it converts that name into her actual phone number. DNS converts a website name into its IP address.

How Websites Actually Work

Simple Definition: When you type a website name, your computer must first find that site's IP address (like 142.250.x.x), then open a connection to that address, send an HTTP request, and receive the page back. Names are for humans; IP addresses are for machines. DNS is the translation step that makes this possible before anything else can happen.

Root Server

Simple Definition: The root servers sit at the very top of DNS. They don't know individual websites, but they know where to find the servers responsible for each top-level domain (.com, .org, etc.). There are 13 root server "identities" worldwide (mirrored across many machines). Every lookup can start here.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA library's main directory that doesn't list every book but tells you which section (floor) to go to.

TLD (Top-Level Domain)

Simple Definition: The TLD is the last part of a domain — .com, .org, .in, .io. TLD servers know which name server is responsible for each domain under them. So the .com TLD server knows where to find info about google.com.

Name Server (Authoritative)

Simple Definition: The authoritative name server is the final source of truth for a specific domain. It actually holds the records (like the IP address) for google.com. When the resolver reaches it, it gives the real answer.

DNS Resolution Process (Step-by-Step)

sequenceDiagram participant U as 💻 Your Computer participant R as 🔁 Resolver (ISP) participant Root as 🌐 Root Server participant TLD as 🏷️ .com TLD Server participant NS as 📇 Authoritative NS U->>R: What is the IP of google.com? R->>Root: Where is .com? Root-->>R: Ask the .com TLD server R->>TLD: Where is google.com? TLD-->>R: Ask google's name server R->>NS: What is google.com's IP? NS-->>R: 142.250.182.78 R-->>U: 142.250.182.78 (cached for TTL) U->>U: Now connect to that IP
A full recursive DNS lookup. After this, the answer is cached so it's instant next time.
  1. You type google.com. Your computer asks a resolver (usually your ISP's or 8.8.8.8).
  2. Resolver asks a root server → it points to the .com TLD server.
  3. Resolver asks the TLD server → it points to google's authoritative name server.
  4. Resolver asks the name server → it returns the IP address.
  5. Resolver caches the answer and gives it to your computer, which then connects.

DNS Caching

Simple Definition: To avoid doing that whole journey every time, DNS answers are cached (remembered) at several layers — your browser, your OS, and the resolver. Most lookups are answered instantly from cache. This massively reduces traffic and speeds up browsing.

TTL in DNS

Simple Definition: TTL (Time To Live) is how long a cached DNS answer stays valid, in seconds. A TTL of 3600 means "trust this for 1 hour, then re-check." Low TTL = changes propagate fast but more lookups; high TTL = fewer lookups but slow to update.

⚠️ Interview TipBefore migrating servers, lower the TTL days in advance so the new IP spreads quickly when you switch.

DNS Load Balancing

Simple Definition: DNS can return different IP addresses for the same name to spread users across multiple servers or data centers. Simple "round-robin DNS" rotates through a list of IPs. It's a basic, cheap form of load balancing at the very first step.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA pizza chain's single phone number that automatically routes your call to the nearest branch.

Anycast DNS

Simple Definition: Anycast lets many servers around the world share the same IP address; the network automatically routes you to the nearest/healthiest one. This makes DNS (and CDNs) fast and resilient — if one location fails, traffic flows to another with no change needed.

Real-World Tech Example: Cloudflare's 1.1.1.1 and Google's 8.8.8.8 use Anycast so you hit a nearby server.

📌 Key TakeawayDNS = phonebook of the internet. A lookup walks Root → TLD → Name Server, then everything is cached for the TTL. Anycast + DNS load balancing make it fast and highly available worldwide.

Module 06

Networking & Edge Infrastructure

This module covers how data physically travels across the internet, how it's kept secure, and the "edge" pieces (CDN, API Gateway) that sit between users and your servers.

TCP/IP Basics

Simple Definition: TCP/IP is the set of rules computers use to talk over the internet. IP handles addressing and routing packets to the right machine (like the postal address on an envelope). TCP ensures those packets arrive completely, in order, and without errors — it resends anything lost. Together they create a reliable connection from small packets traveling separate paths.

💡 Real-Life ExampleMailing a long letter as numbered pages (packets): IP delivers each envelope; TCP makes sure all pages arrive and you read them in order, requesting re-sends for any missing page.

There's also UDP, a faster but unreliable alternative (no resends), used for live video/gaming where speed beats perfection.

HTTP vs HTTPS

Simple Definition: HTTP is the language browsers and servers use to exchange web pages (requests and responses). HTTPS is the same thing but encrypted, so nobody snooping on the network can read or tamper with the data. The "S" stands for Secure, provided by TLS.

💡 Real-Life ExampleHTTP is a postcard anyone can read; HTTPS is a sealed, tamper-proof envelope.

TLS/SSL Encryption

Simple Definition: TLS (the modern name; SSL is its old name) is the technology that encrypts HTTPS connections. Through a quick "handshake," the browser and server agree on secret keys and verify the server's identity using a certificate. After that, all data is scrambled so only the two parties can read it.

The TLS handshake: verify identity, agree on a secret, then encrypt everything.

Forward Proxy vs Reverse Proxy

Simple Definition: A forward proxy sits in front of clients and makes requests on their behalf (hiding/filtering users). A reverse proxy sits in front of servers and receives requests on their behalf (hiding/protecting servers, doing load balancing, TLS, caching). Same idea (a middleman), opposite side.

💡 Real-Life ExampleForward proxy = a personal shopper who buys things for you so the store never sees you. Reverse proxy = a company receptionist who takes all incoming calls before routing them inside.

CDN (Content Delivery Network)

Simple Definition: A CDN is a worldwide network of cache servers that store copies of your static content close to users, so it loads fast. (Introduced in Module 1; here's the detail.)

flowchart LR U1["👤 User in India"] --> E1["📍 Edge (Mumbai)"] U2["👤 User in USA"] --> E2["📍 Edge (New York)"] E1 -->|"cache miss only"| O[("🏢 Origin Server")] E2 -->|"cache miss only"| O
Users hit the nearest edge; the origin is contacted only on a cache miss.

Edge Servers

The CDN's many servers placed in cities worldwide ("the edge"). They serve cached content to nearby users in milliseconds, reducing distance and load on your origin.

Cache Invalidation

The process of removing or refreshing outdated copies on edge servers when content changes. Done via TTL expiry or an explicit "purge." Getting this right avoids serving users old files.

Origin Server

Your real, central server that holds the original content. The CDN fetches from it only when its cache is empty or stale (a "cache miss"), then keeps a copy.

Static vs Dynamic Content Delivery

Static content (images, CSS, JS, videos) is the same for everyone and caches beautifully on CDNs. Dynamic content (your personalized feed) differs per user and is harder to cache, though modern CDNs can cache parts of it or run logic at the edge.

Real-World Tech Example: Cloudflare, Akamai, and AWS CloudFront power most of the web's media delivery.

API Gateway

Simple Definition: The single entry point that receives all API requests and handles shared concerns before passing them to backend services. (Introduced in Module 1; details below.)

flowchart TD C["📱 Client"] --> G["🚪 API Gateway
(auth · rate limit · routing · transform)"] G --> S1["👤 User Service"] G --> S2["📦 Order Service"] G --> S3["💳 Payment Service"]

Routing and Aggregation

Routing sends each request to the correct service. Aggregation lets the gateway call several services and combine their responses into one, so the client makes a single call instead of many.

Authentication at the Gateway

Checking the caller's identity (token/API key) once at the gateway, so individual services don't each re-implement login checks.

Rate Limiting at the Gateway

Capping how many requests a client can make in a time window, applied centrally to protect all services from abuse and overload.

Request/Response Transformation

Reshaping data as it passes through — e.g., converting formats, adding headers, or hiding internal fields — so clients and services can evolve independently.

📌 Key TakeawayTCP/IP moves data reliably; TLS keeps it private (HTTPS). At the edge, a reverse proxy / CDN brings content close to users, and an API Gateway centralizes auth, rate limiting, routing, and transformation.

Module 07

APIs and Communication

Systems need to talk — clients to servers, services to services. The "language and style" they use to talk is an API and a communication protocol. Here are all the main options and when to use each.

What is an API

Simple Definition: An API (Application Programming Interface) is a contract that lets two pieces of software talk. It defines what requests you can make and what responses you'll get back, without exposing the messy internals. You call a defined "endpoint," send some data, and receive a result. APIs are how apps use each other's features.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA restaurant menu: you don't enter the kitchen, you just order from a defined list and food arrives. The menu is the API.

REST APIs

Simple Definition: REST is the most common API style for the web. It treats everything as a "resource" (users, orders) with a URL, and uses standard HTTP methods (GET, POST, etc.) to act on them. Data is usually exchanged as JSON. It's simple, stateless, and works everywhere. (Deep dive in Module 8.)

Real-World Tech Example: Most public APIs — Twitter, Stripe, GitHub — are REST.

SOAP APIs

Simple Definition: SOAP is an older, stricter, XML-based protocol with formal contracts (WSDL) and built-in standards for security and reliability. It's heavier and more rigid than REST, but its strictness suits banking and enterprise systems that need guarantees.

Real-World Tech Example: Many banks and legacy enterprise/payment systems still use SOAP.

GraphQL

Simple Definition: GraphQL is a query language for APIs where the client asks for exactly the fields it needs in one request — no more, no less. This avoids "over-fetching" (getting too much) and "under-fetching" (needing many calls). There's usually a single endpoint.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA buffet where you put exactly the items you want on your plate, vs a fixed thali where you get whatever they decided.

Real-World Tech Example: Facebook created it; GitHub and Shopify offer GraphQL APIs.

gRPC

Simple Definition: gRPC is a high-performance way for services to call each other, using a compact binary format (Protocol Buffers) over HTTP/2. It's much faster and smaller than JSON, supports streaming, but isn't human-readable and is awkward directly from browsers. Ideal for internal service-to-service calls.

Real-World Tech Example: Google, Netflix, and Uber use gRPC between microservices.

WebSockets

Simple Definition: WebSockets open a single, persistent two-way connection between client and server, so both can send messages anytime instantly — no repeated requests. Perfect for real-time apps where the server must push updates immediately.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA phone call (open line, both can talk anytime) vs sending letters back and forth (HTTP request/response).

Real-World Tech Example: WhatsApp Web, live chat, multiplayer games, and trading apps.

Server-Sent Events (SSE)

Simple Definition: SSE lets a server push a continuous stream of updates to the client over one HTTP connection, but in one direction only (server → client). It's simpler than WebSockets when the client just needs to receive live updates and doesn't need to send much back.

Real-World Tech Example: Live news tickers, stock price feeds, notification streams.

Long Polling

Simple Definition: A trick for near-real-time updates using normal HTTP: the client asks the server and the server holds the request open until it has new data, then responds; the client immediately asks again. It mimics push without special protocols, but is less efficient than WebSockets/SSE.

💡 Real-Life ExampleCalling a shop and them keeping you on hold until your item is ready, then telling you — instead of you calling every minute.

Webhooks

Simple Definition: A webhook is a "reverse API" — instead of you repeatedly asking a service for updates, the service calls your URL when an event happens. You register a URL; they POST data to it on events. Great for event notifications between systems.

💡 Real-Life ExampleInstead of checking the mailbox every hour, you ask the postman to ring your doorbell when a package arrives.

Real-World Tech Example: Stripe sends a webhook to your server when a payment succeeds; GitHub fires webhooks on a push.

Sync vs Async Communication

Simple Definition: In synchronous communication the caller waits for the response before continuing. In asynchronous communication the caller sends the request and moves on, getting the result later (often via a queue or callback). Sync is simple and immediate; async is resilient and great for slow or bursty work.

REST API Deep Dive

REST is the workhorse of web APIs, so it deserves its own module. Here's everything you need to design clean, correct REST APIs.

HTTP Methods (GET, POST, PUT, PATCH, DELETE)

Simple Definition: HTTP methods (also called "verbs") tell the server what action you want on a resource. Each has a clear meaning, and using them correctly makes your API predictable.

MethodMeaningExampleIdempotent?
GETRead data (no changes)Get a user's profileYes
POSTCreate a new resourceCreate a new orderNo
PUTReplace a resource fullyReplace entire profileYes
PATCHUpdate part of a resourceChange just the emailUsually
DELETERemove a resourceDelete an orderYes
💡 Real-Life ExampleThink of a document: GET = read it, POST = create a new one, PUT = rewrite the whole thing, PATCH = edit one line, DELETE = shred it.

Endpoints and Resource Naming

Simple Definition: An endpoint is the URL that represents a resource. Good REST uses nouns (not verbs) and plurals, with hierarchy showing relationships. The HTTP method supplies the action, so the URL just names the thing.

✅ Good:  GET /users/42/orders        (orders of user 42)
✅ Good:  POST /users                 (create a user)
❌ Bad:   GET /getUserOrders?id=42    (verb in URL)
❌ Bad:   POST /createNewUser         (action in URL)

JSON

Simple Definition: JSON (JavaScript Object Notation) is the standard text format for sending data in REST APIs. It's human-readable, made of key–value pairs, lists, and nested objects. Almost every language can read and write it.

{
  "id": 42,
  "name": "Asha",
  "email": "asha@example.com",
  "roles": ["user", "admin"]
}

Query Parameters

Simple Definition: Query parameters are extra key–value options added after a ? in the URL to filter, sort, search, or paginate — without changing which resource you're hitting. They're for refining a GET request.

GET /products?category=shoes&sort=price&limit=20&page=2

Request and Response Body

Simple Definition: The request body carries the data you send (usually with POST/PUT/PATCH) — like the new user's details. The response body carries the data the server sends back — like the created resource or an error message. GET requests typically have no body.

Status Codes (2xx, 3xx, 4xx, 5xx)

Simple Definition: A status code is a 3-digit number the server returns to say how the request went. The first digit gives the category.

RangeMeaningCommon codes
2xxSuccess200 OK, 201 Created, 204 No Content
3xxRedirection301 Moved, 304 Not Modified
4xxClient's fault400 Bad Request, 401 Unauthorized, 403 Forbidden, 404 Not Found, 429 Too Many Requests
5xxServer's fault500 Internal Error, 502 Bad Gateway, 503 Unavailable
⚠️ Interview TipRemember: 4xx = "you messed up" (fix your request), 5xx = "we messed up" (server problem). Returning the right code is part of good API design.

API Design Best Practices

  • Use nouns + plurals for resources; let HTTP methods be the verbs.
  • Return correct status codes and consistent error formats.
  • Version your API from day one.
  • Paginate large lists; never return everything at once.
  • Secure with HTTPS, auth, and rate limits.
  • Keep responses predictable and documented.

Idempotency

Simple Definition: An operation is idempotent if doing it many times has the same effect as doing it once. GET, PUT, DELETE are naturally idempotent; POST usually isn't (it creates a new thing each time). This matters because networks fail and clients retry — idempotency prevents duplicates.

💡 Real-Life ExamplePressing an elevator button 5 times = same result as once (idempotent). Withdrawing $100 five times = very different (not idempotent).

Pagination

Simple Definition: Pagination breaks a large list into smaller "pages" so you don't fetch millions of rows at once. It keeps responses fast and memory-friendly.

Offset-based Pagination

Uses ?limit=20&offset=40 ("skip 40, give 20"). Simple and lets you jump to any page, but gets slow on huge datasets and can show duplicates/skips if data changes between pages.

Cursor-based Pagination

Uses a pointer to the last item seen: ?limit=20&cursor=abc123 ("give 20 after this item"). Fast and stable even as data changes, but you can't jump to an arbitrary page. Best for infinite-scroll feeds.

API Versioning

Simple Definition: Versioning lets you change an API without breaking existing apps that rely on the old behavior. You publish v2 while v1 keeps working.

URL Versioning

Put the version in the path: /v1/users, /v2/users. Very visible and easy to route; the most common approach.

Header Versioning

Put the version in a request header (e.g., Accept: application/vnd.api.v2+json). Keeps URLs clean but is less obvious and harder to test in a browser.

Rate Limiting in APIs

Simple Definition: Rate limiting caps how many requests a client can make in a time window (e.g., 100/minute) to prevent abuse, ensure fairness, and protect the system. Exceeding it returns 429 Too Many Requests. (Algorithms in Module 20.)

💡 Real-Life ExampleAn amusement-park ride that lets only a fixed number of people on per minute so it doesn't get overwhelmed.
📌 Key TakeawayGood REST = noun-based URLs, correct HTTP methods & status codes, idempotency for safe retries, pagination for big lists, versioning for change, and rate limiting for protection.

Module 09

SQL Databases

SQL (relational) databases organize data into neat tables with strict rules. They've powered serious applications for decades because they keep data accurate and let you ask powerful questions.

Relational Databases

Simple Definition: A relational database stores data in tables that can be linked ("related") to each other. You interact with it using SQL (Structured Query Language). It enforces a fixed structure (schema) and strong rules so data stays consistent. It's the default choice when correctness and relationships matter.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA well-organized Excel workbook where each sheet is a table, rows are records, and sheets reference each other by ID.

Real-World Tech Example: PostgreSQL, MySQL, Oracle — used for banking, e-commerce orders, and user accounts.

Tables, Rows, Columns

Simple Definition: A table holds one type of thing (e.g., Users). Each row is one record (one user). Each column is one attribute (name, email). Every row has a value for each column, giving a clean grid.

flowchart TB subgraph Users["📋 Users Table"] direction LR H["id | name | email"] R1["1 | Asha | asha@x.com"] R2["2 | Ravi | ravi@x.com"] end

Constraints

Simple Definition: Constraints are rules the database enforces to keep data valid — like NOT NULL (must have a value), UNIQUE (no duplicates), and CHECK (value must satisfy a condition). They stop bad data from ever being saved.

Primary Key and Foreign Key

Simple Definition: A primary key is a column that uniquely identifies each row (like a user's id) — no duplicates, never empty. A foreign key is a column in one table that points to the primary key of another, creating a link. Together they connect tables reliably.

💡 Real-Life ExampleYour Aadhaar/SSN is a primary key (uniquely you). An order form listing your ID is a foreign key pointing back to you.

Joins and Relationships

Simple Definition: A join combines rows from two tables based on a related column, letting you answer questions that span tables (e.g., "show each order with its customer's name"). Relationships describe how tables connect.

One-to-One

Each row in A matches exactly one row in B. Example: a user and their single passport record.

One-to-Many

One row in A links to many rows in B. Example: one customer has many orders. (The most common relationship.)

Many-to-Many

Rows in A link to many in B and vice versa, handled with a "join table." Example: students and courses — each student takes many courses, each course has many students.

One-to-one, one-to-many, and many-to-many relationships.

ACID Properties

Simple Definition: ACID is the set of guarantees that make SQL transactions trustworthy. They ensure your data is never left in a broken, half-done state.

  • Atomicity — All steps of a transaction happen, or none do. A money transfer either debits and credits, or nothing changes.
  • Consistency — A transaction moves the database from one valid state to another, never breaking rules/constraints.
  • Isolation — Concurrent transactions don't mess each other up; each behaves as if running alone.
  • Durability — Once committed, data survives crashes and power loss (it's written to permanent storage).
💡 Real-Life ExampleBank transfer: Atomicity = no money vanishes mid-transfer; Durability = once the receipt prints, it's permanent even if the power fails.

Transactions

Simple Definition: A transaction groups several operations into one all-or-nothing unit. If any step fails, the whole group is rolled back. This is how databases keep multi-step operations safe.

Isolation Levels

Simple Definition: Isolation levels control how much transactions can "see" each other's in-progress work. Higher isolation = more correctness but less concurrency (slower); lower = faster but risks anomalies.

LevelPreventsTrade-off
Read UncommittedAlmost nothing (can read "dirty" uncommitted data)Fastest, least safe
Read CommittedDirty readsCommon default
Repeatable ReadDirty + non-repeatable readsStronger
SerializableEverything (acts fully sequential)Safest, slowest

Indexing

Simple Definition: An index is an extra data structure that lets the database find rows fast without scanning the whole table — like a book's index. It dramatically speeds up reads on indexed columns but slightly slows writes and uses extra space.

💡 Real-Life ExampleFinding "Mitochondria" via a textbook's index in seconds vs flipping through every page.

B-tree Index

The default, all-purpose index. Keeps data sorted, so it's great for equality and range queries (>, <, BETWEEN, sorting).

Hash Index

Maps keys to locations via hashing. Very fast for exact-match lookups (=), but useless for ranges or sorting.

Composite Index

An index on multiple columns together (e.g., (last_name, first_name)). Helps queries filtering on those columns in order. Column order matters a lot.

Index Trade-offs

Indexes speed reads but cost write performance (each write updates indexes) and storage. Over-indexing hurts; index the columns you actually filter/sort/join on.

⚠️ Interview Tip"Add an index" is the #1 first answer for a slow query — but mention the write/storage cost to show depth.

Normalization vs Denormalization

Simple Definition: Normalization splits data into many tables to avoid duplication (each fact stored once) — great for write integrity. Denormalization deliberately duplicates data into fewer tables to make reads faster (fewer joins). It's a classic read-speed vs write-simplicity trade-off.

NormalizedDenormalized
Data duplicationMinimalIntentional
ReadsSlower (joins)Faster (fewer joins)
Writes/updatesSimple, safeMust update copies
Best forOLTP, integrityRead-heavy, analytics

Connection Pooling

Simple Definition: Opening a new database connection is expensive. A connection pool keeps a set of ready-made connections that requests borrow and return, instead of creating one each time. This boosts performance and protects the database from too many connections.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA pool of taxis waiting at a stand — you grab one and return it, rather than building a new car for each trip.

Database Migrations and Schema Evolution

Simple Definition: As an app grows, its tables must change (new columns, new tables). Migrations are versioned, repeatable scripts that safely apply these schema changes across environments. They let the database evolve without manual, error-prone edits.

📌 Key TakeawayChoose SQL when you need relationships, complex queries, and ACID guarantees. Master primary/foreign keys, transactions, isolation levels, and indexing — they appear in nearly every backend.

NoSQL Databases

NoSQL ("Not Only SQL") databases trade some of SQL's strictness for flexibility and massive scale. They come in several types, each built for a different shape of data.

Why NoSQL is Needed

Simple Definition: As apps reached billions of users and huge, fast-changing data, the rigid schema and single-machine tendencies of SQL became painful. NoSQL databases were built to scale out across many machines easily, handle flexible/unstructured data, and deliver very high read/write throughput. They relax some guarantees to gain speed and scale.

Scaling Challenges with SQL

Simple Definition: SQL databases are hard to scale horizontally because joins, transactions, and strict consistency are tough to maintain across many machines. They scale up (bigger box) more easily than out (more boxes). When one machine isn't enough, NoSQL's design makes spreading data simpler.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA single perfectly organized library is easy to run, but splitting one collection across 100 buildings while keeping perfect cross-references is hard — that's SQL at scale.

Key-Value Databases (Redis, DynamoDB)

Simple Definition: The simplest NoSQL type: store a value under a unique key, like a giant dictionary. Lookups by key are blazing fast. Great for caching, sessions, and simple lookups; not for complex queries.

Real-World Tech Example: Redis (in-memory cache), Amazon DynamoDB (managed, massive scale).

Document Databases (MongoDB)

Simple Definition: Store data as flexible JSON-like "documents," each of which can have its own structure. Perfect when data is nested and varies between records (product catalogs, user profiles). You can query inside documents.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA folder of forms where each form can have different fields — no single rigid template required.

Graph Databases (Neo4j)

Simple Definition: Built to store and traverse relationships — nodes (things) connected by edges (relationships). Ideal when connections are the main point: social networks, recommendations, fraud rings. Finding "friends of friends" is fast and natural.

Real-World Tech Example: LinkedIn-style connections, fraud detection, recommendation engines.

Columnar Databases (Cassandra, HBase)

Simple Definition: Store data by columns rather than rows, and distribute across many nodes. Excellent for huge write volumes and analytical reads over specific columns. Designed for scale and availability across data centers.

Real-World Tech Example: Cassandra powers Netflix and Instagram's high-write workloads.

Time-Series Databases

Simple Definition: Specialized for data stamped with time — metrics, sensor readings, stock prices — where you mostly append new points and query by time ranges. Optimized for fast writes and time-based aggregation.

Real-World Tech Example: InfluxDB, TimescaleDB, Prometheus for monitoring metrics.

BASE Properties

Simple Definition: BASE is NoSQL's relaxed counterpart to ACID, favoring availability and scale over immediate consistency.

  • Basically Available — The system stays responsive even during failures (it always answers, perhaps with slightly stale data).
  • Soft state — Data may be "in flux"/changing in the background, not guaranteed perfectly settled at every instant.
  • Eventual consistency — If writes stop, all copies will eventually agree, but not instantly.
💡 Real-Life ExampleA like count that briefly shows 1,000 to you and 1,002 to a friend, then syncs to the same number a second later — fine for likes, not for bank balances.
📌 Key TakeawayPick the NoSQL type that matches your data shape: key-value for lookups/caching, document for flexible records, graph for relationships, columnar for huge writes, time-series for metrics. Accept BASE/eventual consistency where strict ACID isn't required.

Storage Systems

Not all storage is a "database." Files, videos, backups, and search indexes need different kinds of storage. Here are the main types and when to use each.

Block Storage

Simple Definition: Block storage splits data into fixed-size chunks ("blocks") and presents them as a raw disk that an operating system or database can format and use. It's very fast and low-level, like a hard drive attached to your server. You build file systems or databases on top of it.

💡 Real-Life ExampleAn empty bookshelf with numbered slots — you decide how to organize the books.

Real-World Tech Example: AWS EBS, used as the disk for databases and VMs.

File Storage

Simple Definition: File storage organizes data as files inside folders (a hierarchy), accessed over a network so many machines can share it. It's familiar and easy, like a shared network drive. Good for shared documents and content, but harder to scale to internet size.

Real-World Tech Example: Network drives, AWS EFS, Google Drive-style shares.

Object / Blob Storage (S3-style)

Simple Definition: Object storage keeps each file as an "object" with a unique key and metadata, in a flat space (no real folders). It scales almost infinitely, is cheap, and is accessed over HTTP APIs. Perfect for images, videos, backups, and static assets — but you can't edit part of an object; you replace the whole thing.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA valet parking system: you hand over your car (object) and get a ticket (key); you don't care where it's parked, you just retrieve it by ticket.

Real-World Tech Example: Amazon S3 stores virtually all of Netflix's and Dropbox's media.

Storage Tiers (Hot, Warm, Cold)

Simple Definition: Cloud storage comes in tiers by access frequency and price. Hot = accessed often, fast, pricey. Warm = occasional access, cheaper. Cold/archive = rarely accessed, very cheap, but slow to retrieve. Moving old data to colder tiers saves a lot of money.

Real-World Tech Example: S3 Standard (hot) → S3 Infrequent Access (warm) → S3 Glacier (cold).

Data Warehouse Basics

Simple Definition: A data warehouse is a large store optimized for analytics (OLAP) — combining data from many sources to run big reports and find trends. It's tuned for reading huge volumes, not for live transactions. Data is usually loaded via ETL pipelines (Module 18).

Real-World Tech Example: Snowflake, Google BigQuery, Amazon Redshift.

Search Engines and Indexing (Elasticsearch-style)

Simple Definition: A search engine builds special indexes so you can find documents by their content quickly, even across millions of records. The core trick is an inverted index: a map from each word to the list of documents containing it. This makes text search nearly instant.

💡 Real-Life ExampleThe index at the back of a book: look up a word and instantly get every page it appears on — instead of reading the whole book.

Real-World Tech Example: Elasticsearch powers search and log analytics at Uber, GitHub, and many others.

Full-Text Search vs Structured Query

Simple Definition: A structured query (SQL) filters on exact fields ("price < 100 AND category = shoes"). Full-text search understands language — matching words, handling typos, ranking by relevance ("blu runing shoez" still finds blue running shoes). Use structured queries for precise data, full-text search for human language.

📌 Key TakeawayUse block storage for databases, file storage for shared files, and object storage for media/backups at scale. Move cold data to cheaper tiers, use a data warehouse for analytics, and a search engine (inverted index) for fast text search.

Caching

Caching is the single most effective performance technique in system design. A cache stores copies of data in fast memory so repeated requests are answered instantly instead of hitting the slow database every time.

💡 Real-Life ExampleKeeping your most-used books on your desk instead of walking to the library each time. The desk is the cache.

Why Cache is Important

Simple Definition: Databases and disks are relatively slow; memory (RAM) is thousands of times faster. By keeping frequently used data in a cache, you cut response times from tens of milliseconds to under a millisecond, and you take huge load off the database. This improves speed, scale, and cost all at once. The challenge is keeping cached copies fresh.

Cache Hit and Cache Miss

Simple Definition: A cache hit is when the data you want is already in the cache (fast!). A cache miss is when it isn't, so you must fetch it from the database and usually store it in the cache for next time. The percentage of hits is the "hit ratio" — higher is better.

The cache hit/miss flow.

TTL (Time To Live)

Simple Definition: TTL is how long a cached item stays valid before it's automatically discarded. A short TTL keeps data fresh but causes more misses; a long TTL is faster but risks staleness. It's the simplest way to expire cache entries.

Caching Strategies (Read & Write Patterns)

StrategyHow it worksTrade-off
Read ThroughApp asks cache; on miss, the cache itself loads from DB and returns itSimple reads; first read is slow
Write ThroughEvery write goes to cache and DB at the same timeAlways fresh; slower writes
Write AroundWrites go straight to DB, skipping cache; cache filled on later readsAvoids caching rarely-read data; first read is a miss
Write BackWrites go to cache first, flushed to DB later in batchesVery fast writes; risk of data loss if cache fails before flush

Read Through Cache

The application always talks to the cache; the cache is responsible for fetching missing data from the database. Keeps app code simple.

Write Through Cache

Data is written to the cache and database together, so the cache is never stale — at the cost of slightly slower writes.

Write Around Cache

Writes bypass the cache and go to the database; the cache only fills when that data is later read. Avoids polluting the cache with data nobody reads.

Write Back (Write Behind) Cache

Writes hit the cache instantly and are saved to the database asynchronously later. Fastest writes, but you can lose recent writes if the cache crashes before flushing.

Cache Eviction Policies

Simple Definition: A cache has limited space, so when it's full it must remove ("evict") something to make room. The eviction policy decides what to throw out.

  • LRU (Least Recently Used) — Evict the item not used for the longest time. Most popular and usually a great default.
  • LFU (Least Frequently Used) — Evict the item used the fewest times overall. Good when popularity is stable.
  • FIFO (First In First Out) — Evict the oldest-inserted item, regardless of use.
  • MRU (Most Recently Used) — Evict the most recently used item (useful in rare scan patterns).
  • LIFO (Last In First Out) — Evict the most recently inserted item.
💡 Real-Life ExampleLRU = cleaning your fridge by tossing the food you haven't touched in the longest time.

Distributed Caching

Simple Definition: When one cache server isn't enough, you spread the cache across many machines, using consistent hashing to decide which server holds which key. This gives huge cache capacity and high availability. Redis Cluster and Memcached do this.

Cache Invalidation Strategies

Simple Definition: Invalidation is removing or updating stale cache entries when the underlying data changes. Options include TTL expiry, deleting the key on write, or updating the cache on write. It's famously hard — "there are only two hard things in computer science: cache invalidation and naming things."

⚠️ Interview TipA common, robust pattern is "cache-aside + delete on write": on update, delete the cache key so the next read repopulates fresh data.

CDN as a Cache Layer

Simple Definition: A CDN (Module 6) is essentially a giant geographic cache for static content, sitting closest to users. It's the first caching layer for images, videos, and files, cutting both latency and origin load.

📌 Key TakeawayCache hot, read-heavy data in fast memory; choose a write strategy (through/around/back) based on freshness vs speed needs; use LRU eviction and a solid invalidation plan; scale with distributed caching and front everything static with a CDN.

Load Balancer

A load balancer distributes incoming traffic across multiple servers so no single server is overwhelmed and the system stays up if a server dies. It's the gateway that makes horizontal scaling work.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA supermarket receptionist sending each customer to whichever checkout counter is free, keeping all lines balanced.

Why Load Balancers Are Needed

Simple Definition: One server can only handle so much traffic, and if it fails the whole app goes down. A load balancer spreads requests across many servers, removes failed ones from rotation, and gives clients a single stable address. This delivers both scalability (handle more) and availability (survive failures). It's the foundation of any multi-server system.

Layer 4 vs Layer 7 Load Balancing

Simple Definition: Layer 4 balances based on network info (IP and port) without looking at the content — fast and simple. Send each new request to the next server in order, looping around. Simple and fair when servers are equal.

Health Checks

Simple Definition: The load balancer regularly pings each server (e.g., a /health endpoint) to confirm it's alive and responding. Unhealthy servers are automatically removed from rotation and added back when they recover. This is what makes failover automatic.

Sticky Sessions (Session Affinity)

Simple Definition: Sticky sessions tie a user to one specific server for their whole session, so server-stored session data stays accessible. It's a workaround for stateful servers, but it hurts even load distribution and breaks if that server dies. Prefer stateless servers with shared session storage instead.

⚠️ Interview TipIf asked about sticky sessions, mention the better alternative: keep servers stateless and store sessions in Redis, so any server can serve any user.

Replication

Replication means keeping copies of your data on multiple machines. It improves availability (if one copy dies, others survive), read performance (spread reads across copies), and disaster recovery. The tricky part is keeping copies in sync.

💡 Real-Life ExamplePhotocopying an important document and storing copies in several safes — if one safe is destroyed, you still have the data.

Single Leader Replication

Simple Definition: One node is the leader (handles all writes); the others are followers (replicas) that copy the leader's changes and serve reads. Writes are simple and consistent because they go to one place, but the leader is a write bottleneck and a single point of failure (mitigated by failover). This is the most common setup.

flowchart TD W["✍️ Writes"] --> L["👑 Leader"] L -->|"replicate"| F1["📄 Follower 1 (reads)"] L -->|"replicate"| F2["📄 Follower 2 (reads)"] R["👁️ Reads"] --> F1 R --> F2
Single-leader: all writes to the leader, reads spread across followers.

Multi-Leader Replication

Simple Definition: Multiple nodes can accept writes (often one leader per region), then sync with each other. This improves write availability and lets each region write locally (low latency), but creates conflicts when the same data is changed in two places at once, which you must resolve.

Real-World Tech Example: Used for multi-region apps and collaborative editing where each region needs fast local writes.

Leaderless Replication

Simple Definition: There's no leader; the client (or a coordinator) writes to several nodes directly and reads from several too. Consistency is achieved by reading/writing to enough nodes (quorum). It's highly available and fault-tolerant. Dynamo and Cassandra use this style.

Sync vs Async Replication

Simple Definition: In synchronous replication the leader waits for replicas to confirm before telling the client "done" — safer (no data loss) but slower. In asynchronous replication the leader replies immediately and replicates in the background — faster but you can lose recent writes if the leader crashes before they're copied.

Quorum Reads and Writes

Simple Definition: In leaderless systems, a quorum is a minimum number of nodes that must agree. With N copies, if writes go to W nodes and reads check R nodes, and W + R > N, then reads are guaranteed to see the latest write. It's a tunable knob between consistency and availability.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA committee of 5 where any decision needs at least 3 votes — overlapping majorities guarantee everyone hears the latest decision.

Read Replicas

Simple Definition: Read replicas are follower copies dedicated to serving read queries, taking load off the primary. They're the standard way to scale read-heavy applications. Writes still go to the leader; reads fan out to replicas.

Replication Lag and Its Impact

Simple Definition: Replication lag is the delay before a write on the leader appears on the replicas. During this gap, a replica may return stale data — e.g., you post a comment but don't see it because your read hit a lagging replica. This causes confusing "read-your-writes" problems (Module 16).

⚠️ Interview TipTo avoid users not seeing their own writes, route their reads to the leader for a short time after they write, or pin them to an up-to-date replica.

Cross-Region Replication

Simple Definition: Copying data across data centers in different geographic regions, so users worldwide get low latency and the system survives a whole-region outage. The challenge is that distance increases lag and conflict potential, and it raises cost.

📌 Key TakeawaySingle-leader is the safe default (writes to leader, reads to replicas). Use multi-leader/leaderless + quorums for multi-region availability. Always account for replication lag — it's the source of many "I don't see my data" bugs.

Partitioning (Sharding)

When data is too big for one machine, you split it across many — this is partitioning, or "sharding." Each shard holds a slice of the data. Replication makes copies; partitioning splits the data itself.

💡 Real-Life ExampleSplitting one huge warehouse into several smaller warehouses by product category — electronics here, clothing there — so each is manageable.

Partitioning splits one big dataset into smaller, independent shards.

Partition by Key (Range Partitioning)

Simple Definition: Data is split by ranges of a key — e.g., users A–H on shard 1, I–P on shard 2. Range queries ("all users L–N") are efficient because related data sits together. The downside: ranges can become unbalanced, creating hotspots (e.g., lots of names starting with 'S').

Partition by Hash

Simple Definition: Run the key through a hash function and use the result to pick a shard. This spreads data evenly and avoids hotspots, but you lose efficient range queries because related keys scatter across shards. Great when you mostly look things up by exact key.

Consistent Hashing for Sharding

Simple Definition: Consistent hashing (from Module 4) places shards on a ring so that adding or removing a shard moves only a small slice of data, not everything. It's the standard way to shard so the cluster can grow/shrink smoothly. Virtual nodes are added to keep the load even.

Secondary Index Partitioning

Simple Definition: The shard is usually chosen by the primary key, but you often need to query by other fields (e.g., find users by city). Secondary indexes let you do that, and they too must be partitioned. There are two ways to do it (below).

Global vs Local Secondary Indexes

  • Local index — Each shard indexes only its own data. Writes are simple (one shard), but a query by the secondary field must ask every shard ("scatter-gather"), which is slower to read.
  • Global index — One index across all shards, itself partitioned by the indexed field. Reads are fast (go to one place), but writes are more complex (must update an index possibly on another shard).
💡 Real-Life ExampleLocal = asking every branch library if they have a book. Global = one central catalog that tells you exactly which branch has it.

Hotspots and Challenges

Simple Definition: A hotspot (or "hot shard") is when one shard gets far more traffic than others — e.g., a celebrity's data, or all writes hitting the "newest" shard. It defeats the purpose of sharding. Fixes include better key choice, adding randomness to keys, or splitting the hot key.

⚠️ Interview TipChoosing a good shard key is the most important decision. A bad key (like timestamp for write-heavy data) creates hotspots. Pick a key with high cardinality and even access.

Rebalancing Shards

Simple Definition: Over time shards grow unevenly or you add machines, so data must be redistributed — "rebalancing." Good schemes (like consistent hashing or fixed virtual partitions) move minimal data and avoid downtime. Poorly planned rebalancing can overload the network and the cluster.

📌 Key TakeawayPartition to scale beyond one machine. Hash partitioning spreads load evenly; range partitioning enables scans. Pick the shard key carefully to avoid hotspots, and use consistent hashing so rebalancing moves little data.

CAP Theorem & Consistency Models

When data lives on multiple machines, you face fundamental trade-offs about consistency and availability. CAP and the consistency models explain what you can and cannot guarantee.

The CAP Theorem

Simple Definition: CAP says a distributed data store can guarantee at most two of three properties at the same time during a network failure: Consistency, Availability, and Partition tolerance. Since network failures (partitions) are unavoidable, you really must choose between Consistency and Availability when one happens.

Consistency

Every read sees the most recent write — all nodes show the same data at the same time.

Availability

Every request gets a (non-error) response, even if some nodes are down — but it might be slightly stale.

Partition Tolerance

The system keeps working even when the network between nodes breaks and messages are lost.

💡 Real-Life ExampleDuring a phone outage between two bank branches: CP = refuse withdrawals until they reconnect (stay correct). AP = allow withdrawals at both (stay open, risk inconsistency).

Trade-offs in Distributed Systems

Because partitions will happen, the real choice is CP vs AP. Banks lean CP (correctness over uptime); social feeds and shopping carts lean AP (uptime over perfect freshness). There's no universally "right" answer — it depends on what's worse for your users: wrong data or no data.

PACELC Theorem

Simple Definition: PACELC extends CAP: if there's a Partition (P), choose Availability or Consistency (A/C); Else (E), during normal operation, choose between Latency and Consistency (L/C). It captures that even without failures, you trade speed for consistency.

⚠️ Interview TipPACELC shows maturity: "Even with no partition, stronger consistency costs latency." E.g., DynamoDB is PA/EL by default (favors availability and low latency).

Consistency Models

These describe what guarantees a system gives about how fresh and ordered your reads are — from strictest to loosest.

Strong Consistency

Every read instantly reflects the latest write, everywhere. Simplest to reason about, but slower and less available. Needed for money and inventory.

Eventual Consistency

Reads may be stale for a while, but if writes stop, all copies eventually converge. Fast and highly available; fine for likes, view counts, feeds.

Causal Consistency

Operations that are causally related (a reply must come after its message) are seen in the right order by everyone, while unrelated operations can be seen in any order. A practical middle ground.

Read-Your-Writes Consistency

You always see your own writes immediately (even if others see them later). Prevents the "I posted but it vanished" experience.

Monotonic Reads and Writes

Monotonic reads: once you've seen a newer value, you'll never later see an older one (no going backward in time). Monotonic writes: your writes are applied in the order you made them.

Session Consistency

Guarantees like read-your-writes and monotonic reads hold within a single user session. A popular, practical guarantee that gives a smooth personal experience without full strong consistency.

💡 Real-Life ExampleRead-your-writes = after you change your profile photo, you see it immediately even if friends see the old one for a minute.
📌 Key TakeawayDuring a network partition you must choose Consistency or Availability (CAP); even normally, consistency costs latency (PACELC). Pick the weakest consistency model that still gives a good user experience — strong for money, session/eventual for feeds.

Message Queue

A message queue lets parts of a system communicate by passing messages, so slow work can happen in the background and components don't depend on each other being available at the same moment.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA restaurant's order-ticket rail: waiters clip up orders and move on; the kitchen works through tickets at its own pace. The rail is the queue.

Sync vs Async Processing

Simple Definition: In synchronous processing, the user waits while the work finishes. In asynchronous processing, the request is accepted, dropped into a queue, and the user gets an instant "we've got it" while workers do the heavy job later. Async keeps apps responsive and absorbs spikes.

flowchart LR P["📤 Producer
(web server)"] --> Q["📩 Message Queue"] Q --> C1["⚙️ Consumer/Worker 1"] Q --> C2["⚙️ Consumer/Worker 2"] C1 --> DB[("🗄️ DB")]
Producers add messages; independent consumers process them at their own pace.

FIFO Queue

Simple Definition: First-In-First-Out: messages are processed in the exact order they arrived. Important when order matters, like processing bank transactions in sequence. It can limit parallelism since strict order must be preserved.

Priority Queue

Simple Definition: Messages have priorities, and higher-priority ones are processed first regardless of arrival order. Useful when some tasks are urgent (a paid-user request) and others can wait (a routine email).

Pub/Sub (Publish/Subscribe)

Simple Definition: Publishers send messages to a "topic," and multiple subscribers each receive a copy. It's one-to-many broadcasting, decoupling senders from receivers — the publisher doesn't know or care who's listening. Great for fan-out events.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA newspaper publisher: they print one edition (publish), and every subscriber gets their own copy delivered.

Real-World Tech Example: Kafka, Google Pub/Sub, and RabbitMQ power event broadcasting across services.

Push vs Pull Model

Simple Definition: In push, the queue actively sends messages to consumers as they arrive (low latency, but can overwhelm slow consumers). In pull, consumers request messages when they're ready (consumer controls pace, better backpressure). Kafka uses pull; many traditional brokers push.

Poison Messages

Simple Definition: A poison message is one that repeatedly fails processing (maybe it's malformed), causing endless retries that clog the system. The fix is to detect repeated failures and move it aside (to a dead letter queue) instead of retrying forever.

Duplicate Messages

Simple Definition: Due to retries and network issues, the same message can be delivered more than once. Consumers must handle this — usually by being idempotent (processing twice = same result) or tracking processed message IDs.

Message Ordering Guarantees

Simple Definition: Whether messages are guaranteed to arrive in the order sent. Strict global ordering limits scalability, so systems like Kafka guarantee order only within a partition. You design keys so related messages share a partition and stay ordered.

Dead Letter Queues (DLQ)

Simple Definition: A separate queue where messages that can't be processed (after several retries) are parked for later inspection. It keeps bad messages from blocking the main queue and gives engineers a place to debug failures.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA "return to sender / problem mail" bin at the post office for letters that can't be delivered, instead of jamming the main sorting line.

Data Processing

Once you've collected data, you need to process it — to compute reports, power analytics, or react to events. Processing comes in two main styles (batch and stream) and several pipeline patterns.

Batch Processing

Simple Definition: Collect a large amount of data and process it all together at scheduled times (hourly, nightly). It's efficient and simple for huge volumes where instant results aren't needed, like generating daily reports. The downside is latency — results aren't fresh.

💡 Real-Life ExampleDoing all your week's laundry in one big load on Sunday.

Real-World Tech Example: Hadoop and Spark batch jobs for nightly analytics.

Stream Processing

Simple Definition: Process each event the moment it arrives, continuously, giving near-instant results. It's needed when freshness matters — fraud detection, live dashboards, alerting. It's more complex than batch because the data never stops.

💡 Real-Life ExampleWashing each dish right after you use it, instead of piling them up.

Real-World Tech Example: Apache Flink, Kafka Streams, Spark Streaming for real-time analytics.

ETL Pipelines (Extract, Transform, Load)

Simple Definition: ETL moves data from sources into a warehouse for analysis. Extract pulls raw data from databases/APIs, Transform cleans and reshapes it, and Load stores it in the warehouse. (A newer variant, ELT, loads first then transforms inside the warehouse.)

💡 Real-Life ExampleCooking: gather ingredients (extract), chop and cook them (transform), plate the dish (load).

MapReduce Concept

Simple Definition: MapReduce is a model for processing huge datasets across many machines in two steps. Map breaks the work into pieces and processes each in parallel (producing key-value pairs); Reduce combines the results by key. It made "big data" processing on commodity machines possible.

Real-Time Analytics

Simple Definition: Computing metrics and insights on data as it flows in, so dashboards and decisions reflect the present, not yesterday. Built on stream processing, it powers live counts, trending topics, and instant alerts.

Event-Driven Data Pipelines

Simple Definition: Instead of polling on a schedule, components react to events (a new order, a click) as they happen, often via a message log like Kafka. Each stage consumes events, processes, and emits new events. This makes pipelines flexible, decoupled, and real-time.

Fault Tolerance & High Availability

Things fail — disks die, code crashes, people make mistakes. Fault tolerance is designing so the system keeps working even when parts fail. High availability is the result: the service stays up.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA plane with multiple engines: if one fails, the others keep it flying safely to a landing.

Types of Failures

Hardware Failures

Disks, servers, network cards, and whole data centers eventually fail. At scale, failures are constant, so designs assume hardware will break and add redundancy.

Software Failures

Bugs, memory leaks, crashes, and bad deployments. These can cascade across machines (a bad release everywhere), so safe deploys and isolation matter.

Human Errors

The most common cause of outages — wrong config, accidental deletes, fat-finger commands. Mitigate with automation, reviews, staged rollouts, and easy rollback.

Recovery and Reliability

Simple Definition: Reliability is the system doing the right thing despite faults; recovery is how it returns to normal after a failure. Techniques include retries, replication, backups, and automated failover. The goal is to make failures invisible (or at least brief) to users.

Active-Active vs Active-Passive

Simple Definition: In active-active, multiple nodes handle traffic at once, so a failure just shifts load to the rest (no downtime, but more complex). In active-passive, a standby waits idle and takes over only when the active node fails (simpler, but there's a brief switch-over delay and idle resources).

Failover Strategies

Simple Definition: Failover is automatically switching to a healthy backup when the primary fails. Good failover detects failure quickly (health checks), promotes a replica, and redirects traffic — ideally within seconds and without losing data. Poorly tested failover is a top cause of outages.

Redundancy Patterns

Simple Definition: Redundancy means having spare copies of every critical component (servers, databases, network paths, power) so no single failure takes you down. The core principle is eliminating "single points of failure" (SPOFs).

⚠️ Interview TipWhen asked to improve availability, hunt for the single point of failure first — one load balancer, one database, one region — and add redundancy there.

Disaster Recovery (RPO & RTO)

Simple Definition: Disaster recovery is your plan for big failures (a region goes down). Two key metrics define your goals:

  • RPO (Recovery Point Objective) — How much data you can afford to lose, measured in time. RPO of 5 min = you may lose the last 5 minutes of data. Drives backup/replication frequency.
  • RTO (Recovery Time Objective) — How long you can afford to be down. RTO of 1 hour = you must be back within an hour. Drives failover automation.
💡 Real-Life ExampleRPO = how often you save your document (lose work since last save). RTO = how fast you can get a working computer again after yours dies.

Multi-Region Architecture

Simple Definition: Running your system in multiple geographic regions so a whole-region outage doesn't take you offline, and users get low latency from a nearby region. It's the gold standard for availability — but costs more and adds data-sync complexity.

Geo-Replication

Simple Definition: Continuously copying data across regions so each region has a usable copy. It enables multi-region reads and disaster recovery, but introduces replication lag and possible conflicts (see Module 14/16).

Chaos Engineering Basics

Simple Definition: Deliberately breaking parts of your system in production (in a controlled way) to discover weaknesses before real failures do. By regularly "killing" servers, you prove your redundancy and failover actually work. Netflix pioneered this with "Chaos Monkey."

💡 Real-Life ExampleA fire drill — you start small controlled fires to make sure the alarms, sprinklers, and exits all work before a real fire.
📌 Key TakeawayAssume everything fails. Remove single points of failure with redundancy, use health-check-driven failover, define RPO/RTO targets, go multi-region for top availability, and verify it all with chaos testing.

Idempotency Keys

Simple Definition: A unique key the client attaches to a request so that if it's accidentally sent twice (due to a retry), the server recognizes the duplicate and performs the action only once. Essential for safe retries in payments and orders.

Real-World Tech Example: Stripe requires an Idempotency-Key header so a retried charge doesn't bill the customer twice.

Distributed Locks

Simple Definition: A lock that works across multiple machines, ensuring only one process does a critical action at a time (e.g., only one worker processes a job). Often built with Redis or ZooKeeper. Tricky because the lock holder might crash, so locks need timeouts.

Simple Definition: A process where distributed nodes agree on one "leader" to coordinate work, and automatically elect a new one if it fails. Prevents conflicting decisions. Tools like ZooKeeper, etcd, and Raft handle this.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA group project automatically picking a team lead, and instantly choosing a new one if the lead leaves.

Circuit Breaker Pattern

Simple Definition: Like an electrical breaker, it "trips" to stop calling a failing service after too many failures, returning errors instantly instead of waiting and piling up. After a cooldown it tests with a few requests; if healthy, it closes again. This prevents cascading failures.

Retry with Exponential Backoff

Simple Definition: When a request fails, retry it — but wait progressively longer between attempts (1s, 2s, 4s, 8s…), ideally with random "jitter." This gives the struggling service time to recover and prevents a stampede of synchronized retries.

⚠️ Interview TipAlways pair retries with backoff + jitter and a max attempt count. Naive instant retries can turn a small blip into a full outage (a "retry storm").

Bulkhead Pattern

Simple Definition: Isolate resources into separate "compartments" so a failure in one doesn't sink the whole system — like watertight bulkheads in a ship. For example, give each downstream dependency its own thread pool so one slow service can't exhaust all threads.

Timeout and Deadline Propagation

Simple Definition: Every remote call should have a timeout so it doesn't wait forever. Deadline propagation passes a shared deadline down a chain of services, so once the overall time budget is spent, every service stops working on that doomed request instead of wasting effort.

Backpressure and Flow Control

Simple Definition: When a component is overwhelmed, backpressure is its way of signaling upstream to slow down (or pause), instead of collapsing. It keeps the system stable under load by matching intake to capacity.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA kitchen telling waiters to stop taking orders when it's swamped, rather than accepting orders it can never cook.

Saga Pattern for Distributed Transactions

Simple Definition: A way to keep data consistent across multiple services without a single global transaction. A saga is a sequence of local transactions; if one step fails, it runs compensating actions to undo the previous steps. Used in microservices where each service has its own database.

💡 Real-Life ExampleBooking a trip: book flight → book hotel → book car. If the car fails, you cancel the hotel and flight (compensations) to undo the trip.

Two-Phase Commit (2PC)

Simple Definition: A protocol to commit a transaction across multiple databases atomically. Phase 1: a coordinator asks all participants "can you commit?" Phase 2: if all say yes, it tells everyone to commit; otherwise everyone aborts. It guarantees all-or-nothing but is slow and blocks if the coordinator fails — so sagas are often preferred at scale.

📌 Key TakeawayProtect against overload (rate limiting, backpressure), failure (circuit breaker, retry+backoff, bulkhead, timeouts), and coordinate safely (idempotency keys, distributed locks, leader election). For cross-service consistency, prefer sagas over 2PC at scale.

Architecture Styles

How you structure your whole application — as one big unit or many small ones — shapes everything: speed of development, scalability, and reliability. Here are the main styles and patterns.

Monolithic Architecture

Simple Definition: The entire application is built and deployed as one single unit — all features in one codebase, one process, one database. It's simple to build, test, and deploy early on. But as it grows, it becomes hard to change, scale selectively, or develop in parallel; one bug can crash everything.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA Swiss Army knife — everything in one tool. Convenient, but you can't upgrade just the scissors.

Microservices Architecture

Simple Definition: The app is split into many small, independent services, each owning one capability (users, payments, search) and its own database, communicating over the network. Teams develop, deploy, and scale each service independently. The trade-off is operational complexity — networking, monitoring, and data consistency get harder.

Real-World Tech Example: Netflix and Amazon famously moved from monoliths to hundreds/thousands of microservices.

Serverless Architecture

Simple Definition: You write functions and the cloud runs them on demand, automatically scaling and charging only for actual usage — you never manage servers. Great for spiky or event-driven workloads and fast development. Downsides: cold-start latency, vendor lock-in, and limits on long-running tasks.

Real-World Tech Example: AWS Lambda, used for image processing, webhooks, and lightweight APIs.

Event-Driven Architecture

Simple Definition: Services communicate by emitting and reacting to events ("OrderPlaced") rather than calling each other directly. This decouples components — producers don't know who consumes — making the system flexible and scalable. It's harder to trace and debug flows, though.

Service-Oriented Architecture (SOA)

Simple Definition: An older predecessor to microservices: the app is built from reusable services, often communicating through a central "enterprise service bus." Services tend to be larger and more shared than microservices, and the central bus can become a bottleneck.

Database per Service Pattern

Simple Definition: In microservices, each service owns its own private database; no other service touches it directly. This keeps services independent and loosely coupled, but means cross-service queries and transactions need APIs or sagas instead of joins.

⚠️ Interview TipIf microservices share one database, you don't really have microservices — you have a distributed monolith with all the pain and none of the benefits.

CQRS (Command Query Responsibility Segregation)

Simple Definition: Separate the model for writing data (commands) from the model for reading it (queries), often using different data stores optimized for each. Reads can be denormalized for speed while writes stay clean. It adds complexity, so use it only where read and write needs differ greatly.

Event Sourcing

Simple Definition: Instead of storing only the current state, store every change as an immutable sequence of events. The current state is rebuilt by replaying events. This gives a full audit history and time-travel, but is more complex and queries need projections. Often paired with CQRS.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA bank statement: instead of just "balance = $500," you keep every deposit and withdrawal, and the balance is the sum of them.

Strangler Fig Pattern

Simple Definition: A safe way to migrate a monolith to microservices gradually. You put a proxy in front, then slowly route specific features to new services while the old system handles the rest, until the monolith is fully replaced. Named after a vine that slowly grows over and replaces a tree.

Service Discovery

Simple Definition: In microservices, services come and go and change addresses, so they need a way to find each other. A service registry keeps a live directory; services look up where a dependency currently lives. Tools: Consul, Eureka, Kubernetes DNS.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA constantly updated company phone directory, so you can always reach a colleague even if they switched desks.

Inter-Service Communication (Sync vs Async)

Simple Definition: Services talk either synchronously (direct request/response, e.g., REST/gRPC — simple but creates tight coupling and chained failures) or asynchronously (via events/queues — resilient and decoupled but eventually consistent). Most real systems mix both, using sync for immediate needs and async for everything else.

📌 Key TakeawayStart monolithic for simplicity; move to microservices only when scale and team size demand it (use the strangler fig to migrate). Give each service its own DB, communicate async where possible, and reach for CQRS/event sourcing only when the complexity pays off.

Security & Identity

Security protects your users and data. The two core questions are "who are you?" (authentication) and "what are you allowed to do?" (authorization), plus protecting data in transit and at rest.

Authentication vs Authorization

Simple Definition: Authentication (AuthN) verifies who you are (login with password, fingerprint). Authorization (AuthZ) decides what you're allowed to do once identified (can you delete this file?). Authentication always comes first; authorization follows.

💡 Real-Life ExampleAt an airport: showing your passport is authentication (proving who you are); your boarding pass is authorization (which flight/seat you may board).

OAuth 2.0

Simple Definition: A standard that lets you grant one app limited access to your data on another service without sharing your password. The service gives the app a temporary access token with specific permissions. This powers "Login with Google/Facebook."

💡 Real-Life ExampleA hotel key card: the front desk (Google) gives you a card (token) that opens only your room and the gym (specific scopes), not the whole hotel — and it expires.

JWT (JSON Web Tokens)

Simple Definition: A compact, signed token that carries user info (claims) and can be verified without a database lookup. After login, the server issues a JWT; the client sends it with each request, and the server trusts it because the signature proves it wasn't tampered with. It enables stateless authentication.

⚠️ Interview TipJWTs can't be easily revoked before they expire, so keep their lifetime short and use refresh tokens. Never put secrets in a JWT — its contents are readable (just signed, not encrypted).

SSO (Single Sign-On)

Simple Definition: Log in once and access many related applications without logging in again. A central identity provider authenticates you and vouches for you to each app. Improves user experience and centralizes security.

Real-World Tech Example: Logging into Gmail also signs you into YouTube, Drive, and Docs.

RBAC (Role-Based Access Control)

Simple Definition: Permissions are grouped into roles (admin, editor, viewer), and users are assigned roles. You manage access by role rather than per-user, which scales well. Simple and the most common authorization model.

ABAC (Attribute-Based Access Control)

Simple Definition: Access decisions use attributes of the user, resource, and context (department, time of day, location, ownership). More flexible and fine-grained than RBAC, but more complex to manage. Example: "editors can edit only documents in their own department, during work hours."

Encryption at Rest vs in Transit

Simple Definition: In transit = data encrypted while moving over networks (via TLS/HTTPS), so eavesdroppers can't read it. At rest = data encrypted while stored on disk/database, so stolen drives or backups are useless without keys. You need both for real protection.

💡 Real-Life ExampleIn transit = an armored truck carrying cash. At rest = a locked vault storing it.

HTTPS and Certificate Management

Simple Definition: HTTPS uses TLS certificates to prove a site's identity and encrypt traffic. Certificates are issued by trusted authorities, expire, and must be renewed/rotated. Mismanaged (expired) certificates are a frequent, embarrassing cause of outages.

API Key Management

Simple Definition: API keys identify and authenticate applications calling your API. They must be kept secret, rotated regularly, scoped to least privilege, and revocable if leaked. Never hard-code keys in client code or commit them to source control.

Secrets Management

Simple Definition: Passwords, API keys, and certificates ("secrets") should be stored in a dedicated, encrypted vault — not in code or config files. Tools provide access control, rotation, and audit logs. This prevents leaked credentials, a top breach cause.

Real-World Tech Example: HashiCorp Vault, AWS Secrets Manager.

Common Security Threats

  • SQL Injection — Attackers sneak malicious SQL into inputs to read/alter your database. Fix: use parameterized queries, never build SQL by concatenating user input.
  • XSS (Cross-Site Scripting) — Attackers inject malicious JavaScript that runs in other users' browsers. Fix: escape/sanitize output and use Content Security Policy.
  • CSRF (Cross-Site Request Forgery) — Tricks a logged-in user's browser into making unwanted requests. Fix: use anti-CSRF tokens and SameSite cookies.
💡 Real-Life ExampleSQL injection = slipping a fake instruction into a form that the clerk blindly carries out. CSRF = someone forging a check using your already-signed checkbook.
📌 Key TakeawayAuthenticate (who) then authorize (what) — RBAC for most, ABAC for fine-grained. Encrypt data in transit (HTTPS) and at rest, store secrets in a vault, rotate keys/certs, and defend against SQLi, XSS, and CSRF with proven techniques.

Monitoring and Observability

You can't fix what you can't see. Monitoring tells you that something is wrong; observability helps you understand why. Together they keep systems healthy and incidents short.

Three Pillars: Logs, Metrics, Traces

Simple Definition: Observability rests on three data types. Logs are timestamped text records of discrete events ("user 42 failed login"). Metrics are numeric measurements over time (CPU %, requests/sec). Traces follow a single request as it travels through many services, showing where time is spent. You need all three.

flowchart LR O["🔭 Observability"] --> L["📝 Logs
(what happened)"] O --> M["📈 Metrics
(how much / how fast)"] O --> T["🧵 Traces
(where time went)"]
💡 Real-Life ExampleLogs = a diary of events; Metrics = your fitness tracker's numbers; Traces = a GPS route showing exactly where you got stuck.

API Monitoring

Simple Definition: Continuously watching your APIs for availability, response time, and errors, often with synthetic test requests. It catches problems before users complain. The core API metrics are throughput, latency, and error rate (below).

Throughput

Simple Definition: How many requests the system handles per unit of time (e.g., requests per second). It measures capacity. Watching throughput reveals traffic patterns and whether you're nearing limits.

Latency (p50, p95, p99)

Simple Definition: Latency is how long a request takes. Averages hide pain, so we use percentiles: p50 (median) = typical experience; p95 = the slowest 5% of requests; p99 = the slowest 1%. The "tail" (p95/p99) reveals the bad experiences real users hit.

⚠️ Interview TipAlways quote tail latency (p99), not just the average. "Average is fine" can hide that 1 in 100 users waits 5 seconds.

Error Rates

Simple Definition: The percentage of requests that fail (typically 5xx responses). A rising error rate is one of the clearest signals of trouble and a common trigger for alerts. Tracked alongside latency and throughput as the "golden signals."

Health Checks

Simple Definition: Lightweight endpoints (e.g., /health) that report whether a service and its dependencies are working. Load balancers and orchestrators use them to route traffic away from sick instances and restart them.

System Resource Metrics

Simple Definition: Beyond the app, you monitor the machine's vital signs to spot bottlenecks:

  • CPU Usage — How busy the processor is; sustained high CPU means compute is the bottleneck.
  • Memory Usage — RAM consumed; running out causes crashes or slow swapping.
  • Disk I/O — Read/write activity to storage; a bottleneck for data-heavy apps and databases.
  • Network I/O — Data flowing in/out; saturated networks cause latency and timeouts.

Distributed Tracing

Simple Definition: Following one request across many microservices by attaching a shared trace ID, so you can see the full journey and pinpoint which service caused the slowdown or error. Essential in microservices where one click touches dozens of services. Tools: Jaeger, Zipkin, OpenTelemetry.

Alerting Strategies

Simple Definition: Rules that notify engineers when metrics cross dangerous thresholds. Good alerting is actionable and rare — alert on user-facing symptoms (high error rate, slow latency), not every minor blip, to avoid "alert fatigue" where people ignore alarms.

SLA, SLO, SLI

Simple Definition: Three related reliability terms, from measurement to promise:

  • SLI (Indicator) — The actual measured number (e.g., "99.95% of requests succeeded").
  • SLO (Objective) — Your internal target for that indicator (e.g., "99.9% success").
  • SLA (Agreement) — A formal promise to customers, with penalties if broken (e.g., "99.9% uptime or we refund you").
💡 Real-Life ExampleSLI = your actual exam score; SLO = the score you aim for; SLA = a deal with your parents that has consequences if you miss it.
flowchart LR SLI["📏 SLI (measured)"] --> SLO["🎯 SLO (target)"] --> SLA["📜 SLA (promise + penalty)"]

On-Call and Incident Response Basics

Simple Definition: Engineers take turns being "on-call" to respond to alerts 24/7. When an incident hits, the flow is: detect → acknowledge → mitigate (stop the bleeding) → resolve → run a blameless postmortem to learn and prevent recurrence. The goal is fast recovery and continuous improvement, not blame.

📌 Key TakeawayCombine logs, metrics, and traces for full observability. Track the golden signals (latency at p99, throughput, errors) plus resource metrics, alert only on actionable symptoms, and define SLIs/SLOs/SLAs to make reliability measurable.

Specialized Data Structures & Techniques

Some clever data structures solve "impossible at scale" problems by trading a little accuracy or space for massive speed and memory savings. These show up constantly in real large-scale systems.

Bloom Filters

Simple Definition: A tiny, super-fast structure that tells you whether an item is definitely not in a set or possibly in it. It can have false positives ("maybe") but never false negatives. It uses a fraction of the memory of storing the actual items, perfect for quick "have I seen this?" checks.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA bouncer with a rough memory: "I'm sure that name is NOT on the list" (reliable) or "that name might be on the list, let me double-check" (possible false positive).

Real-World Tech Example: Databases (Cassandra, HBase) use them to skip disk reads for keys that don't exist; web crawlers use them to avoid re-crawling URLs.

flowchart LR X["Check 'item'"] --> H["hash to bits"] --> Q{"All bits set?"} Q -->|"No"| N["✅ Definitely NOT present"] Q -->|"Yes"| M["🤔 Possibly present (verify)"]

HyperLogLog (Cardinality Estimation)

Simple Definition: Counts the number of unique items in a huge stream using tiny memory (kilobytes for billions of items), accepting a small error (~2%). Exactly counting unique visitors would need enormous memory; HyperLogLog estimates it cheaply.

Real-World Tech Example: Redis uses it to count unique visitors; Google/Reddit use it for "unique views" at scale.

Geohashing (Location-Based Systems)

Simple Definition: Encodes a latitude/longitude into a short string where nearby places share similar prefixes. This turns "find things near me" into a fast prefix search instead of comparing distances to everything. Foundational for maps and ride-sharing.

💡 Real-Life ExamplePostal codes: nearby addresses share the same prefix, so you can quickly group locations by area.

Real-World Tech Example: Uber and food-delivery apps use geohashing (and similar grids) to find nearby drivers/restaurants fast.

Count-Min Sketch

Simple Definition: Estimates how frequently each item appears in a stream using small, fixed memory, with a slight overcount possible. Great for finding "heavy hitters" (most frequent items) without storing exact counts for millions of items.

Real-World Tech Example: Finding trending hashtags or top queries in real time.

Merkle Trees (Data Integrity)

Simple Definition: A tree of hashes where each leaf is the hash of a data block and each parent hashes its children, up to one "root hash." Comparing root hashes quickly tells whether two large datasets differ, and you can drill down to find exactly which block changed — without comparing everything.

💡 Real-Life ExampleVerifying two giant books are identical by comparing one "fingerprint" number instead of reading every page.

Real-World Tech Example: Git, blockchains (Bitcoin), and Cassandra/DynamoDB use Merkle trees to detect differences between replicas efficiently.

Skip Lists

Simple Definition: A linked list with extra "express lane" layers that let you skip ahead, giving fast (logarithmic) search, insert, and delete — similar to a balanced tree but simpler to implement. Used where you need ordered data with quick operations.

💡 Real-Life ExampleExpress vs local subway lines: express trains skip stops so you reach a distant station faster, then switch to local for the exact stop.

Real-World Tech Example: Redis uses skip lists for its sorted sets (e.g., leaderboards).

📌 Key TakeawayProbabilistic structures trade tiny error for huge savings: Bloom filters for set membership, HyperLogLog for unique counts, Count-Min Sketch for frequencies. Geohashing powers location search, Merkle trees verify integrity, and skip lists give fast ordered access.

Cost & Trade-off Analysis

Great system design isn't just about what's technically best — it's about what's worth it. Every choice has a cost, and engineers must balance performance, reliability, and money.

Infrastructure Cost Estimation

Simple Definition: Roughly calculating what a system will cost to run — compute (servers), storage, bandwidth (data transfer), and managed services. Like capacity estimation, it uses simple math from your traffic and data assumptions. It prevents nasty surprise bills and guides architecture.

💡 Real-Life ExampleBudgeting a road trip: fuel + tolls + food + hotels. You estimate before you go, not after the bill arrives.

Storage vs Compute Cost Trade-offs

Simple Definition: Often you can trade one resource for another. Precomputing and storing results (more storage) saves repeated calculation (less compute), and vice versa. The right balance depends on which is cheaper for your workload and how often data is read vs recomputed.

💡 Real-Life ExampleCooking meals in bulk and freezing them (storage) vs cooking fresh each time (compute). Bulk saves effort if you eat them often.

Multi-Region Cost Implications

Simple Definition: Running in multiple regions boosts availability and lowers latency, but multiplies cost — duplicate infrastructure, plus expensive cross-region data transfer. You weigh the reliability/latency gains against significantly higher spend, and often go multi-region only for critical workloads.

Caching ROI

Simple Definition: Caching costs money (memory servers) but saves money (fewer database queries, less compute) and improves speed. The "return on investment" is high when data is read far more than it changes. For rarely-read or constantly-changing data, a cache may cost more than it saves.

⚠️ Interview TipCaching is almost always worth it for read-heavy data — but mention the hidden costs: memory spend, stale-data risk, and invalidation complexity.

Build vs Buy Decisions

Simple Definition: Whether to build a capability in-house or use an existing product/managed service. Buying is faster and offloads maintenance but costs subscription fees and gives less control; building gives control and may be cheaper at huge scale but takes time and engineers. Buy commodity pieces, build your unique differentiators.

Cloud Pricing Models (On-Demand, Reserved, Spot)

Simple Definition: Cloud providers sell compute three main ways, trading flexibility for price.

  • On-Demand — Pay per use, no commitment. Most flexible, most expensive. For unpredictable or short-term needs.
  • Reserved — Commit to 1–3 years for a big discount (up to ~70%). For steady, predictable baseline load.
  • Spot — Use spare capacity at huge discounts (up to ~90%), but it can be taken away anytime. For fault-tolerant, interruptible jobs (batch, ML training).
💡 Real-Life ExampleOn-demand = booking a hotel night-by-night; Reserved = a yearly lease at a discount; Spot = last-minute standby airline seats — cheap, but you might get bumped.
📌 Key TakeawayEstimate costs early and design to the budget. Trade storage for compute (and vice versa) wisely, justify caching/multi-region by ROI, buy commodities and build your differentiators, and mix on-demand + reserved + spot to cut cloud bills.

End-to-End System Design Case Studies

Now we combine everything into 12 complete designs. For each: the problem, a full architecture diagram, and the key design decisions. This is exactly how system-design interviews work — connect the building blocks to solve a real product.

26.1 — URL Shortener (TinyURL)

The problem: Turn a long URL into a short one (e.g., tiny.cc/abc123) that redirects to the original. It's extremely read-heavy (far more clicks than creations) and must redirect with very low latency.

flowchart TD U["👤 User"] --> LB["⚖️ Load Balancer"] LB --> API["🖥️ App Servers"] API -->|"create"| KGS["🔢 Key Generation / Counter"] API --> Cache["⚡ Redis Cache (hot links)"] Cache -->|"miss"| DB[("🗄️ DB: shortCode → longURL")] API --> AN["📊 Analytics Queue → Store"]
URL shortener architecture: cache-first redirects, separate key generation, async analytics.

Hash Generation

Two approaches: (1) hash the long URL (e.g., MD5) and take the first few characters — simple but risks collisions; (2) use a global counter and Base62-encode it (0–9, a–z, A–Z) into a short string — collision-free and ordered. Base62 with 7 characters yields ~3.5 trillion unique codes. Most designs prefer the counter approach with a key-generation service.

Redirect Flow

User hits /abc123 → server looks up the code (cache first, then DB) → returns an HTTP 301/302 redirect to the long URL. Caching hot links makes this sub-millisecond.

Analytics Tracking

On each redirect, fire an event (timestamp, location, device) into a message queue so analytics are computed asynchronously without slowing the redirect.

Capacity Estimation

New URLs: 100M/day → ~1,160 writes/sec
Reads (clicks): 100× writes → ~116,000 reads/sec (very read-heavy → cache!)
Storage: 100M/day × 500 bytes × 365 × 5 yrs ≈ 90 TB
Short code: Base62, 7 chars = 62^7 ≈ 3.5 trillion codes

26.2 — Social Media Feed (Twitter / Instagram)

The problem: Show each user a timeline of posts from people they follow, fast, at massive scale. The core challenge is generating billions of personalized feeds efficiently.

flowchart TD P["👤 User posts"] --> WS["✍️ Write Service"] WS --> PDB[("🗄️ Posts DB")] WS --> FO["📤 Fan-out Service"] FO --> TL["⚡ Timeline Cache (per follower)"] R["👁️ User opens app"] --> RS["📖 Read Service"] RS --> TL RS -->|"celebrity posts merged in"| PDB WS --> Media["🖼️ Media → Object Store + CDN"]
Hybrid feed: fan-out-on-write for normal users, fan-out-on-read for celebrities.

Fan-out on Write vs Fan-out on Read

Fan-out on write (push): when you post, copy it into all your followers' precomputed timelines immediately → reads are instant, but a post by someone with millions of followers is hugely expensive to write. Fan-out on read (pull): build the timeline when a user opens the app by fetching recent posts from everyone they follow → cheap writes, but slower reads. Most platforms use a hybrid.

Timeline Generation

Normal users' posts are pushed into followers' Redis timeline caches (fan-out on write). Reads simply fetch the precomputed list — fast.

Celebrity User Problem

Pushing a celebrity's post to 100M followers would overwhelm the system. Solution: don't fan-out celebrity posts; instead, when a user reads their feed, merge in celebrity posts on the fly (fan-out on read). This hybrid keeps both writes and reads manageable.

Media Storage and CDN

Photos/videos go to object storage (S3) and are served via CDN edge servers near users. The feed stores only references (URLs), not the media itself.

26.3 — Chat / Messaging (WhatsApp / Slack)

The problem: Deliver messages instantly and reliably between users, support groups, show who's online, and never lose a message. Needs real-time, persistent connections.

flowchart TD A["📱 Sender"] -->|"WebSocket"| GW["🔌 Connection/Gateway Servers"] GW --> MS["💬 Message Service"] MS --> Q["📩 Queue"] Q --> Store[("🗄️ Message Store")] MS -->|"if online (WebSocket)"| B["📱 Receiver"] MS -->|"if offline"| Push["🔔 Push Notification (APNs/FCM)"] Pres["🟢 Presence Service"] --- GW
Chat architecture using persistent WebSocket connections and a message store for delivery.

One-to-One Messaging

Clients hold a persistent WebSocket connection to gateway servers. A message goes sender → gateway → message service → stored → delivered to the recipient's connection (or push notification if offline). The store guarantees nothing is lost.

Group Chat

The message service looks up all group members and delivers a copy to each (fan-out). For huge groups, this is batched. Each member's unread/delivery state is tracked.

Online Presence

A presence service tracks who's connected via heartbeats over the WebSocket. When a connection drops or misses heartbeats, the user is marked offline. Status changes are pushed to relevant contacts.

Message Delivery Guarantees

Messages get states: sent → delivered → read (WhatsApp's ticks). Acknowledgements and retries with stored state ensure at-least-once delivery; message IDs dedupe duplicates.

End-to-End Encryption Basics

With E2E encryption, messages are encrypted on the sender's device and only decryptable by the recipient — servers relay ciphertext they can't read. WhatsApp uses the Signal Protocol, exchanging public keys so only endpoints hold the decryption keys.

26.4 — Ride Sharing (Uber / Lyft)

The problem: Match riders to nearby drivers in real time, track moving locations continuously, and price dynamically — all with low latency over a city's geography.

flowchart TD D["🚗 Driver app (location every few sec)"] --> LOC["📍 Location Service"] LOC --> GEO["🗺️ Geospatial Index (geohash/QuadTree)"] Rider["🧍 Rider requests ride"] --> Match["🤝 Matching Service"] Match --> GEO Match --> Surge["💲 Surge Pricing Service"] Match --> Trip["🧾 Trip Service"] Trip --> Pay["💳 Payment"]
Ride-sharing core: continuous location updates feed a geospatial index used for matching.

Real-Time Location Tracking

Driver apps send GPS coordinates every few seconds to a location service, which keeps current positions in fast in-memory storage. This high write volume is handled with horizontal scaling and is often kept in memory rather than a traditional DB.

Driver-Rider Matching

When a rider requests, the matching service queries the geospatial index for nearby available drivers, ranks them by ETA, and offers the trip. It must be fast and avoid offering the same driver to two riders (locking).

Surge Pricing

When demand in an area exceeds supply, prices rise to balance them and attract drivers. A pricing service computes a multiplier per geographic zone in near real time based on the supply/demand ratio.

Geospatial Indexing

To find "drivers near me" fast, locations are indexed using geohashing (Module 24) or QuadTrees, turning a proximity search into an efficient lookup instead of scanning every driver.

26.5 — Video Streaming (YouTube / Netflix)

The problem: Let users upload and watch video at massive scale, with smooth playback on any device and network speed. Storage, bandwidth, and global delivery are the challenges.

flowchart TD U["📤 Uploader"] --> UP["Upload Service"] UP --> Raw["🗄️ Raw Video (Object Store)"] Raw --> TR["⚙️ Transcoding Pipeline (queue + workers)"] TR --> Multi["🎞️ Multiple resolutions/bitrates"] Multi --> CDN["🌍 CDN Edge Servers"] V["👁️ Viewer"] --> CDN V --> Rec["🤖 Recommendation Service"] Meta[("Metadata DB")] --- V
Video pipeline: upload → transcode into many formats → distribute via CDN.

Video Upload and Transcoding

Raw uploads land in object storage, then a transcoding pipeline (a queue feeding many workers) converts each video into multiple resolutions and bitrates (240p–4K) and chunks them. This is a compute-intensive batch job done once per video.

Adaptive Bitrate Streaming

The player automatically switches quality based on your current network speed — dropping to lower resolution when bandwidth falls, raising it when it recovers — so playback never stops to buffer. Protocols: HLS, DASH.

CDN for Video Delivery

Encoded chunks are pushed to CDN edge servers worldwide, so your video streams from a nearby server. Netflix even places its own caches inside ISPs. This is what makes global streaming affordable and smooth.

Recommendation System Basics

A recommendation service suggests what to watch next using your history and similar users (collaborative filtering) plus content features. It runs largely offline (batch) and serves precomputed suggestions quickly.

26.6 — E-commerce / Payment System

The problem: Let users browse, add to cart, and pay reliably — without overselling stock or double-charging cards. Correctness and consistency are paramount.

flowchart TD U["🛒 User"] --> GW["🚪 API Gateway"] GW --> Cat["📦 Catalog Service"] GW --> Inv["📊 Inventory Service"] GW --> Order["🧾 Order Service"] Order --> Pay["💳 Payment Service (idempotent)"] Pay --> PG["🏦 External Payment Gateway"] Order --> Q["📩 Events: ship, notify"]
E-commerce as microservices; payment uses idempotency keys to prevent double charges.

Inventory Management

Stock counts must stay accurate under concurrent buyers. Use atomic decrements and reservations (hold stock during checkout with a timeout) so two people can't buy the last item. This often needs strong consistency (SQL transactions).

Order Processing

An order moves through states (created → paid → shipped → delivered). Because it spans services (inventory, payment, shipping), a saga (Module 20) coordinates the steps with compensating actions if any step fails.

Payment Gateway Integration

The payment service calls an external gateway (Stripe/Razorpay) and listens for webhooks confirming success/failure. Sensitive card data is handled by the gateway (PCI compliance), not stored by you.

Idempotency in Payments

Network retries must never double-charge. Each payment carries an idempotency key so the system processes a given charge exactly once, even if the request arrives twice. (See Module 20.)

⚠️ Interview TipFor payments and inventory, always mention idempotency + strong consistency + reservations. This is exactly where eventual consistency is the wrong choice.

26.7 — Notification System

The problem: Send notifications to millions of users across push, email, and SMS — reliably, in the right priority, without spamming anyone. It must handle many channels and huge volume.

flowchart TD Svc["📨 Services trigger notifications"] --> Q["📩 Notification Queue (by priority)"] Q --> Disp["🧭 Dispatcher"] Disp --> Tmpl["📝 Template Service"] Disp --> Pref["⚙️ User Preferences / Rate Limit"] Disp --> Push["🔔 Push (APNs/FCM)"] Disp --> Email["✉️ Email (SES)"] Disp --> SMS["📱 SMS (Twilio)"]
Notification system: queued requests dispatched to the right channel with templates and rate limits.

Push, Email, SMS Channels

Each channel has its own provider and adapter (APNs/FCM for push, SES for email, Twilio for SMS). The dispatcher picks channels based on the notification type and the user's preferences. A clean abstraction lets you add channels easily.

Priority and Batching

Urgent notifications (OTP, security alerts) use a high-priority queue and go instantly; low-priority ones (marketing, digests) are batched and sent together to save cost and reduce noise.

Rate Limiting per User

Cap how many notifications a single user gets in a period so you don't annoy or spam them. Implemented with per-user counters (Module 20 algorithms) and respecting quiet hours.

Template Management

Messages are stored as templates with placeholders ("Hi {name}, your order {id} shipped"). This supports localization and lets non-engineers edit content, while the system fills in user-specific data at send time.

26.8 — Distributed Cache (Redis-like)

The problem: Build a fast key-value cache spread across many nodes, handling huge throughput, surviving node failures, and staying balanced as nodes are added/removed.

flowchart TD C["Clients"] --> Ring["🔵 Consistent Hash Ring"] Ring --> N1["Node 1 (+ replica)"] Ring --> N2["Node 2 (+ replica)"] Ring --> N3["Node 3 (+ replica)"]
Keys are mapped to nodes via consistent hashing; each node has replicas for resilience.

Eviction Policies at Scale

Each node enforces eviction (usually LRU) within its memory limit (Module 12). At scale you tune policies per workload and monitor hit ratios, since a poor policy across thousands of nodes wastes huge memory.

Consistent Hashing for Nodes

Keys map to nodes on a hash ring with virtual nodes, so adding/removing a node moves only a small fraction of keys and load stays even (Module 4). This is what makes the cache cluster elastic.

Cache Stampede Problem

When a popular key expires, thousands of requests miss simultaneously and all hammer the database at once (a "stampede" or "thundering herd"). Fixes: a lock/single-flight so only one request rebuilds the value, slightly randomized TTLs, and serving stale data while refreshing in the background.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA stampede is everyone rushing one ticket counter the instant tickets release; the fix is letting one person fetch for the group while others wait.

26.9 — Search Autocomplete (Typeahead)

The problem: Suggest completions as the user types each letter, within milliseconds, ranked by popularity and relevance. Latency is the dominant constraint.

flowchart TD K["⌨️ User types 'sys'"] --> API["Autocomplete Service"] API --> Trie["🌳 Trie / Prefix Index (in memory)"] Trie --> Rank["📊 Ranking (popularity, personalization)"] Rank --> Out["Top suggestions"] Logs["🔎 Search logs"] -->|"batch build"| Trie
Typeahead: an in-memory prefix tree (trie) returns ranked suggestions per keystroke.

Trie Data Structure

A trie (prefix tree) stores words by shared prefixes, so finding all completions of "sys" is a fast walk down the tree. It's kept in memory for speed, and popular completions can be precomputed at each node.

Ranking and Personalization

Suggestions are ordered by frequency/popularity, recency, and the user's own history/location. Rankings are computed offline from search logs and attached to trie nodes for instant lookup.

Low-Latency Requirements

Because suggestions fire on every keystroke, responses must be a few milliseconds. This demands in-memory data, edge caching, and often debouncing on the client (waiting a beat before querying).

26.10 — Rate Limiter as a Standalone System

The problem: Enforce request limits consistently across many servers and data centers — per user, per IP, and per API — without becoming a bottleneck itself.

flowchart TD R["Incoming request"] --> RL["🚦 Rate Limiter (at gateway)"] RL --> Store["⚡ Central Redis (counters)"] RL -->|"under limit"| Allow["✅ Forward to service"] RL -->|"over limit"| Block["❌ 429 Too Many Requests"]
A shared counter store lets all servers enforce one global limit.

Distributed Rate Limiting

If each server counts separately, a user could exceed the global limit by spreading requests across servers. So counters live in a shared fast store (Redis), giving one consistent view. Atomic operations keep counts accurate under concurrency.

Sliding Window at Scale

The sliding-window-counter algorithm (Module 20) gives smooth, accurate limits with low memory and is implemented atomically in Redis so thousands of servers agree on the count.

Per-User vs Per-IP vs Per-API Limits

Different keys serve different goals: per-user for fairness among accounts, per-IP to stop anonymous abuse, per-API to protect specific expensive endpoints. Real systems combine all three with different thresholds.

26.11 — Web Crawler

The problem: Systematically download billions of web pages to build a search index — politely, without re-visiting the same page, and distributed across many machines.

flowchart TD Seed["🌱 Seed URLs"] --> Frontier["🗂️ URL Frontier (priority queue)"] Frontier --> Fetch["⬇️ Fetcher workers"] Fetch --> Parse["🧩 Parser (extract links)"] Parse --> Dedup["🔎 Bloom filter (seen?)"] Dedup -->|"new URLs"| Frontier Fetch --> Store["🗄️ Page Store → Indexer"]
Crawler loop: fetch → parse → dedupe → enqueue new links, all distributed.

Politeness Policy

The crawler respects each site's robots.txt and limits how often it hits a single domain, so it doesn't overload servers. It spaces out requests per host and honors crawl-delay rules.

URL Frontier

The frontier is the queue of URLs waiting to be crawled, prioritized by importance and freshness, and organized so politeness (per-host rate limits) is enforced. It's the brain that decides what to crawl next.

Duplicate Detection (Bloom Filter)

With billions of URLs, checking "have we seen this?" against a database is too slow. A Bloom filter (Module 24) gives a fast, memory-tiny membership test to skip already-crawled URLs.

Distributed Crawling

Work is split across many machines, typically partitioned by domain (so politeness is easy to enforce per host). A coordinator and shared frontier keep them from duplicating effort.

26.12 — Ticket Booking System (BookMyShow)

The problem: Sell seats for events/movies where many people compete for the same seats at once — without double-booking — and survive massive flash-sale spikes.

flowchart TD U["🧍 Users"] --> Wait["⏳ Virtual Waiting Room (flash sales)"] Wait --> API["🎟️ Booking Service"] API --> Lock["🔒 Seat Lock (Redis, with TTL)"] Lock --> DB[("🗄️ Seats DB (transaction)")] API --> Pay["💳 Payment (idempotent)"] Pay -->|"success"| Confirm["✅ Confirm seat"] Pay -->|"timeout"| Release["🔓 Release seat"]
Booking flow: temporarily lock a seat, confirm on payment, auto-release on timeout.

Concurrency and Locking

Two users must never get the same seat. The system locks a seat the moment one user selects it, using a database transaction or a distributed lock with a timeout, so others see it as unavailable. Strong consistency is essential here.

Seat Reservation

When you pick a seat it's temporarily held (e.g., 5–10 minutes) while you pay. If payment succeeds it's confirmed; if you abandon or time out, the lock auto-expires and the seat returns to the pool.

Handling Flash Sales

For huge launches, a virtual waiting room queues users and admits them in controlled batches, protecting the backend from a sudden stampede. Caching seat-availability views and aggressive rate limiting also help absorb the spike.

📌 Key TakeawayEach case study is just the building blocks (Modules 1–25) recombined. The recurring moves: cache read-heavy data, queue slow/async work, shard for scale, lock + transactions for correctness, and CDN for media. Identify the dominant constraint first, then pick the patterns that solve it.

Real-World Architecture Discussions

Finally, let's look at how famous companies actually built their systems — what specific problem each faced and the clever architecture that solved it. These are the real stories behind the patterns.

Instagram Architecture

The problem: A tiny team had to serve explosive growth of photos and a read-heavy feed with minimal engineers.

The solution: Instagram famously stayed pragmatic — PostgreSQL for core data (users, relationships), sharded across many machines as it grew; Redis/Memcached for heavy caching; object storage + CDN for photos; and a hybrid feed (push for normal users, pull for celebrities). They kept the stack simple and "boring" on purpose, scaling proven tools rather than chasing novelty. The lesson: well-understood components, sharded and cached aggressively, can serve hundreds of millions.

💡 Real-Life ExampleLike running a giant restaurant chain with a small, simple, repeatable menu — easy to scale because it's not over-engineered.

Netflix CDN and Microservices

The problem: Stream video to hundreds of millions globally without buffering, while moving fast as a large engineering org — and survive failures gracefully.

The solution: Two big moves. (1) Open Connect, Netflix's own CDN — purpose-built caching appliances placed inside ISPs, so your show streams from a box in (or near) your ISP. (2) A massive microservices architecture on AWS — hundreds of independently deployable services — with resilience patterns like circuit breakers (Hystrix) and proactive failure testing via Chaos Monkey. This gives both buttery-smooth streaming and high availability despite constant component failures.

flowchart TD V["👁️ Viewer"] --> OC["📦 Open Connect appliance (in ISP)"] V --> MS["🧩 Microservices on AWS"] MS --> CB["🔌 Circuit Breakers"] MS --> CM["🐒 Chaos Monkey (failure testing)"]

WhatsApp Erlang Architecture

The problem: Support hundreds of millions of always-connected users with real-time messaging — using a famously tiny engineering team.

The solution: WhatsApp built on Erlang, a language/runtime designed for telecom: it handles millions of lightweight concurrent processes and persistent connections per server, with built-in fault tolerance ("let it crash" + supervisors). This let a handful of engineers run a service for ~900M users on relatively few servers — each server holding millions of live connections. The lesson: matching the technology to the problem (massive concurrency) can beat brute force.

💡 Real-Life ExampleChoosing the perfect specialized tool — like using a telephone exchange's own technology to run... a messaging service.

Uber Geo-Sharding and Real-Time Matching

The problem: Match riders and drivers in real time across the globe, with constantly moving locations and city-level demand spikes.

The solution: Uber shards data and processing geographically — the world is divided into cells (using systems like Google's S2 / their H3 hexagonal grid), so "find nearby drivers" is a local lookup within a few cells. Real-time location updates flow through high-throughput pipelines (Kafka), and matching/dispatch services operate per region. Surge pricing is computed per geographic zone from live supply/demand. Geo-sharding keeps each query local and fast even at global scale.

flowchart TD W["🌍 World split into hex cells (H3)"] --> C1["Cell shard A: drivers+riders"] W --> C2["Cell shard B: drivers+riders"] C1 --> M["🤝 Local matching + surge"] C2 --> M

Amazon DynamoDB Design

The problem: Amazon needed a database that's always available and scales infinitely for shopping carts and similar workloads — even during failures and Black Friday spikes.

The solution: DynamoDB (born from the Dynamo paper) is a leaderless, key-value/document store that chooses availability over strong consistency (AP in CAP). It uses consistent hashing to spread data, replication across nodes, quorums (tunable W/R) for reads/writes, vector clocks/versioning to detect conflicts, and Merkle trees to sync replicas. The result is predictable single-digit-millisecond latency at any scale, fully managed. It's the textbook example of trading strict consistency for availability and scale.

⚠️ Interview TipDynamoDB ties together half this guide: consistent hashing, replication, quorums, eventual consistency, and Merkle trees. It's a favorite reference design.

Google Search Infrastructure (High Level)

The problem: Index essentially the entire web and answer queries in a fraction of a second for billions of users.

The solution: Google crawls the web (Module 26.11), builds a massive inverted index (word → pages) sharded across thousands of machines, and ranks results (originally PageRank + hundreds of signals today). It pioneered MapReduce (batch processing at scale), GFS/Bigtable (distributed storage), and serves queries by fanning out to many index shards in parallel and merging the top results — all in ~200ms. The lesson: extreme parallelism over distributed indexes plus heavy precomputation.

flowchart TD Q["🔎 Query"] --> Root["Aggregator"] Root --> S1["Index shard 1"] Root --> S2["Index shard 2"] Root --> S3["Index shard 3"] S1 --> Merge["Merge + rank top results"] S2 --> Merge S3 --> Merge Merge --> R["Results (~200ms)"]

Facebook News Feed Ranking (High Level)

The problem: Out of thousands of possible posts, decide which to show each user, in what order, to maximize relevance — instantly, for billions of users.

The solution: Facebook combines fan-out feed generation (Module 26.2) with a ranking model: for each candidate post it predicts how likely you are to engage (like, comment, share, dwell) using ML, then sorts by a relevance score rather than pure chronology. Candidate posts are gathered, scored by a model fed hundreds of signals (relationship, post type, recency, past behavior), and the top ones are cached for fast delivery. Heavy ML scoring happens largely offline/near-real-time so reads stay fast. The lesson: relevance is a machine-learning ranking problem layered on top of a scalable feed pipeline.

💡 Real-Life ExampleA smart personal newspaper editor who, every time you open it, reshuffles all available stories to put the ones you care about on the front page.
📌 Key TakeawayReal architectures aren't magic — they're the same primitives matched to a specific dominant problem: Instagram (simple + sharded + cached), Netflix (own CDN + microservices + chaos), WhatsApp (right language for concurrency), Uber (geo-sharding), DynamoDB (AP + consistent hashing + quorums), Google (parallel inverted index), Facebook (ML ranking on a feed pipeline).
